\begin{abstract}
Early identification of Alzheimer's disease (AD) conversion risk in patients with Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) is critical for clinical trial enrollment and timely therapeutic intervention before irreversible neurodegeneration occurs. We demonstrate that longitudinal slopes of the Boundary Sharpness Coefficient (BSC), a novel MRI biomarker quantifying gray-white matter boundary integrity, allow for (1) prediction of MCI-to-AD conversion using cost-effective structural imaging, (2) identification of temporal neurodegeneration patterns, and (3) discovery of predictive boundary degradation signatures that outperform static baseline measurements. We analyzed 1,824 T1-weighted MRI scans from 450 ADNI subjects (95 converters, 355 stable; mean follow-up 4.84±3.79 years) with $\geq$4 longitudinal scans per subject. BSC voxel-wise maps were computed via Atropos three-class segmentation and directional gradient projection at the GM-WM boundary. For each subject, we extracted 182 BSC features per timepoint (boundary count, directional/magnitude statistics, percentiles, spatial bins), then computed annual rates of change using linear regression across all visits with acquisition dates as temporal anchors. Random Survival Forest (RSF), a non-parametric ensemble learning approach, was trained to predict time-to-AD-conversion using the top 20 most variable BSC slope features, selected to capture the most informative temporal dynamics. The slope-based RSF model achieved a C-index of 0.84 on training data and 0.63 on held-out test data, substantially outperforming baseline-only parametric AFT models (C-index: 0.24), representing a 163\% improvement in predictive accuracy. This non-parametric approach handles complex feature interactions without distributional assumptions, making it robust to the heterogeneity characteristic of AD progression. Key predictive features included slopes of boundary voxel count (Nboundary\_slope: variance=3,160,301), boundary magnitude 90th percentile (bsc\_mag\_p90\_slope: variance=0.0102), and 75th percentile (bsc\_mag\_p75\_slope: variance=0.0073), with converters exhibiting significantly steeper negative slopes. Longitudinal trajectory analysis revealed boundary degradation patterns detectable years before formal AD diagnosis. We show how structural MRI-derived temporal biomarkers can enable AD progression prediction without expensive PET amyloid imaging (\$5,000--\$7,000 per scan) or invasive CSF collection, offering a scalable approach for clinical practice and personalized risk stratification. Future work should validate these slopes in independent cohorts (OASIS, NACC, UK Biobank) and integrate BSC trajectories with cognitive decline rates, genetic risk scores (APOE, polygenic risk), and blood biomarkers (p-tau217, NfL) in multimodal prognostic models.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Alzheimer's disease (AD) is a progressive neurodegenerative disorder affecting over 55 million people worldwide, with projections exceeding 131 million cases by 2050~\cite{hafeez2024deep}. The disease represents the most common cause of dementia, accounting for 60-80\% of all cases, and imposes an estimated global economic burden exceeding \$1 trillion annually. The pathological hallmarks of AD include extracellular amyloid-$\beta$ plaques, intracellular neurofibrillary tau tangles, synaptic dysfunction, neuronal loss, and progressive cognitive decline that ultimately results in complete loss of functional independence.

Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) represents a critical transitional stage between normal aging and dementia, characterized by cognitive deficits beyond what is expected for age and education level, yet insufficient to meet criteria for dementia. Epidemiological studies indicate that approximately 10-15\% of MCI patients convert to AD annually~\cite{li2021predicting}, though conversion rates exhibit substantial heterogeneity depending on underlying pathology, genetic risk factors, and demographic characteristics. This heterogeneity presents both a challenge and an opportunity: while not all MCI patients will progress to AD, early identification of high-risk individuals enables targeted intervention during a window when disease-modifying therapies may have maximal efficacy.

The recent approval of anti-amyloid monoclonal antibodies (aducanumab, lecanemab, donanemab) has intensified the imperative for accurate prognostic biomarkers. These therapies demonstrate efficacy primarily in early-stage disease, making timely identification of at-risk individuals essential for clinical benefit. However, current gold-standard biomarkers present significant barriers to widespread implementation. Amyloid PET imaging, while highly sensitive for detecting cerebral amyloid deposition, costs \$5,000-\$7,000 per scan and remains unavailable in many healthcare systems. Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) analysis of amyloid-$\beta_{42}$, total tau, and phosphorylated tau requires lumbar puncture, an invasive procedure with associated patient discomfort and risk. Blood-based biomarkers (plasma p-tau217, p-tau181, neurofilament light chain) show promise but are not yet standardized for clinical use. Structural magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), in contrast, is widely available, non-invasive, relatively inexpensive, and routinely performed in clinical practice, yet has historically shown only modest predictive performance when using conventional cross-sectional volumetric measurements~\cite{baytas2024predicting}.

\subsection{The Boundary Sharpness Coefficient}

The Boundary Sharpness Coefficient (BSC) is a quantitative MRI-derived biomarker that measures the integrity and sharpness of the cortical gray matter-white matter boundary. Unlike volumetric measures (hippocampal volume, whole-brain atrophy) that quantify tissue loss, or cortical thickness measurements that assess the width of the cortical ribbon, BSC captures \textit{microstructural coherence} at the GM/WM interface. This distinction is important: volumetric and thickness measures reflect macrostructural changes that occur relatively late in disease progression, after substantial neuronal loss has occurred. In contrast, boundary sharpness may be sensitive to earlier subtle alterations in tissue organization, myelin integrity, and synaptic architecture that precede overt atrophy.

The theoretical foundation for BSC as a biomarker rests on the observation that the GM/WM boundary in healthy brain exhibits high MRI contrast due to fundamental biophysical differences between cortical gray matter and subcortical white matter. Gray matter, composed primarily of neuronal cell bodies, dendrites, glial cells, and unmyelinated axons, has relatively long T1 relaxation times and appears hypointense on T1-weighted imaging. White matter, consisting predominantly of organized myelinated axonal fiber bundles, has short T1 relaxation due to lipid-water interactions in myelin sheaths and appears hyperintense. The spatial transition between these compartments is normally abrupt at the microscopic level, producing a sharp intensity gradient that can be quantified on high-resolution MRI.

Pathological processes that disrupt this organization—synaptic loss, dendritic retraction, myelin breakdown, gliosis, inflammatory infiltration—degrade boundary definition. A blurred boundary suggests loss of normal cortical laminar architecture or superficial white matter integrity. Importantly, BSC is \textit{regionally heterogeneous}: primary sensorimotor cortex typically shows sharper boundaries than association cortex, reflecting differences in cytoarchitecture, myelination density, and vulnerability to pathology.

The BSC computation pipeline proceeds through several stages. First, tissue segmentation partitions each voxel into probabilistic tissue class assignments (gray matter probability $P_{GM}$, white matter probability $P_{WM}$, cerebrospinal fluid probability $P_{CSF}$) using Atropos three-class $k$-means clustering. Second, boundary voxels are identified as those where $0.4 \leq P_{GM} \leq 0.6$, forming a thin ribbon at the GM/WM interface. Third, intensity gradients perpendicular to the boundary are computed using Gaussian derivative filters and projected onto boundary normal vectors derived from $\nabla P_{GM}$. Mathematically, for each boundary voxel $\mathbf{x}$, the directional BSC is:

\begin{equation}
    \text{BSC}_{\text{dir}}(\mathbf{x}) = \nabla I(\mathbf{x}) \cdot \frac{\nabla P_{GM}(\mathbf{x})}{\|\nabla P_{GM}(\mathbf{x})\|}
\end{equation}

where $I(\mathbf{x})$ is the T1-weighted intensity at voxel $\mathbf{x}$ and $P_{GM}$ is the gray matter probability map. The gradient $\nabla I$ quantifies the rate of intensity change, and the dot product with the normalized GM probability gradient extracts the component perpendicular to the boundary. Positive values indicate intensity increasing from gray to white (expected direction), while negative values indicate reversed gradients (potentially artifactual). The magnitude $|\text{BSC}_{\text{dir}}|$ provides a scale-invariant measure of boundary sharpness.

This voxel-wise approach yields high-dimensional maps that preserve spatial information, enabling region-specific analyses. However, for survival modeling, we aggregate voxel-wise values into summary statistics (mean, median, standard deviation, percentiles) and spatial bin features that capture boundary degradation patterns in a lower-dimensional feature space amenable to statistical learning.

\subsection{From Static Measures to Temporal Slopes}

A fundamental limitation of cross-sectional neuroimaging biomarkers is their inability to distinguish between long-standing individual differences in brain structure and pathological change attributable to disease progression. Two individuals with identical hippocampal volumes may have vastly different prognoses: one may have always had a small hippocampus (constitutional variation), while the other may have experienced rapid atrophy indicative of aggressive neurodegeneration. This ambiguity can only be resolved through longitudinal assessment.

Recent studies increasingly emphasize the importance of \textit{longitudinal} biomarker trajectories over static baseline measurements. Hafeez et al.~\cite{hafeez2024deep} noted that deep learning models achieved 96\% accuracy in AD classification (a cross-sectional task) but only 84.2\% in MCI conversion prediction (a longitudinal outcome), suggesting that static snapshots fundamentally miss critical temporal dynamics. Similarly, Li et al.~\cite{li2021predicting} demonstrated that molecular subtyping combined with multi-modality imaging improved prediction (AUC 0.83) by recognizing disease heterogeneity, but their approach still relied primarily on baseline features. The concept of \textit{cognitive reserve}—the brain's resilience to pathological damage—further complicates cross-sectional interpretation, as individuals with higher education and occupational attainment may maintain normal cognition despite significant underlying pathology.

These observations converge on a critical insight: \textit{rates of change} may be more informative than absolute values for prognosis. In the context of neurodegenerative disease, trajectory is destiny. Our central hypothesis is that \textbf{longitudinal BSC slopes} (rates of gray-white matter boundary degradation over time) capture disease progression dynamics that are invisible to baseline measurements. A subject with moderate baseline BSC but rapid decline likely has worse prognosis than a subject with low baseline BSC but stable values over time. This approach aligns with emerging consensus in the neuroimaging community to focus on \textit{rates of decline} rather than cross-sectional values when modeling progressive diseases.

\subsection{Study Objectives}

The primary objectives of this study are:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Compute longitudinal BSC slopes for 450 ADNI subjects with $\geq$4 MRI scans
    \item Train Random Survival Forest (RSF) models to predict time-to-AD-conversion using slopes
    \item Compare performance against baseline BSC features and parametric AFT models
    \item Identify which BSC slope features are most predictive of conversion
\end{enumerate}

\section{Materials and Methods}

\subsection{Dataset and Study Population}

Data used in the preparation of this article were obtained from the Alzheimer's Disease Neuroimaging Initiative (ADNI) database (\texttt{adni.loni.usc.edu}). The ADNI was launched in 2003 as a public-private partnership, led by Principal Investigator Michael W. Weiner, MD. The original goal of ADNI was to test whether serial magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), positron emission tomography (PET), other biological markers, and clinical and neuropsychological assessment can be combined to measure the progression of mild cognitive impairment (MCI) and early Alzheimer's disease (AD). The current goals include validating biomarkers for clinical trials, improving the generalizability of ADNI data by increasing diversity in the participant cohort, and to provide data concerning the diagnosis and progression of Alzheimer's disease to the scientific community. For up-to-date information, see \texttt{adni.loni.usc.edu}.

\subsubsection{Inclusion Criteria}

Subjects were included if they had a diagnosis of MCI or Cognitively Normal (CN) at baseline, $\geq$ 4 longitudinal T1-weighted MRI scans with different acquisition dates, scans chronologically ordered with valid visit codes (bl, m12, m24, m36, ...), and complete clinical metadata including diagnosis at each visit. After manifest correction (reordering visits chronologically and relabeling visit codes), 456 subjects met inclusion criteria. The cohort included \textbf{101 converters} who progressed from MCI to AD within the follow-up period, and \textbf{355 stable subjects} who remained MCI or CN and never reached diagnosis=3.0. Six subjects who were diagnosed with AD at their baseline scan (time-to-conversion = 0 years) were excluded from survival analysis, as parametric AFT models require strictly positive event times. Final analysis cohort: \textbf{450 subjects}.

\subsection{MRI Acquisition and Preprocessing}

All subjects underwent T1-weighted structural MRI on either 1.5T or 3T scanners using standardized ADNI protocols. The three-dimensional magnetization-prepared rapid gradient-echo (MPRAGE) sequence employed across sites had typical parameters: repetition time (TR) = 2300 ms, echo time (TE) = 2.98 ms, inversion time (TI) = 900 ms, flip angle = 9°, and isotropic voxel size = 1×1×1 mm³. These parameters were selected by ADNI to optimize gray-white matter contrast while maintaining acceptable scan time (~5-6 minutes per volume) and signal-to-noise ratio.

The BSC computation pipeline consisted of multiple preprocessing stages designed to standardize images and minimize technical artifacts. \textbf{N4 bias correction} addressed intensity inhomogeneity caused by magnetic field non-uniformity, a well-known artifact in MRI that creates slowly varying intensity gradients across the image. Left uncorrected, bias fields would confound BSC measurements by introducing spurious intensity gradients unrelated to tissue boundaries. We employed the N4ITK algorithm, an improved version of the widely-used N4 method, using default convergence criteria (threshold: 0.001) and multi-resolution optimization (4 levels: [50,50,50,50] iterations). \textbf{Skull stripping} removed non-brain tissue (skull, dura, scalp, eyes) that would otherwise contribute boundary artifacts or be misclassified as cortex. We used Otsu thresholding followed by morphological operations (extraction of largest connected component, binary hole filling) to generate brain masks. While more sophisticated deep learning-based skull stripping methods exist (SynthStrip, HD-BET), the Otsu approach provides adequate performance with minimal computational requirements and no need for training data.

Images were \textbf{resampled to isotropic 1 mm³ voxel spacing} using linear interpolation. This standardization ensures consistent spatial resolution across subjects regardless of original acquisition parameters and facilitates voxel-wise analyses. While higher-order interpolation (cubic, sinc) preserves more high-frequency information, linear interpolation avoids introducing ringing artifacts and provides smooth intensity transitions appropriate for gradient computation.

\textbf{Tissue segmentation} using Atropos $k$-means clustering (3 classes) partitioned each voxel into gray matter (GM), white matter (WM), and cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) probability maps. We selected $k$-means over alternative approaches (Gaussian mixture models, hidden Markov random fields, deep learning segmentation) for computational efficiency and reproducibility. The three-class model assumes brain tissue can be adequately represented as a mixture of these major compartments, an approximation that holds reasonably well for T1-weighted imaging. Each voxel receives three probability values summing to 1.0, with $P_{GM}(\mathbf{x})$ representing the probability that voxel $\mathbf{x}$ belongs to gray matter.

\textbf{Boundary identification} selected voxels where $0.4 \leq P_{GM} \leq 0.6$ to form the boundary band. This threshold was chosen to capture the transition zone where gray and white matter probabilities are roughly balanced, operationalizing the GM/WM interface. Alternative definitions (e.g., steepest gradient of $P_{GM}$, or isoprobability contour at exactly 0.5) could be explored but we selected this band definition for stability and reproducibility. \textbf{Gradient computation} applied 3D Gaussian derivative filters with standard deviation $\sigma = 1.0$ mm to calculate spatial derivatives of both intensity $I(\mathbf{x})$ and GM probability $P_{GM}(\mathbf{x})$. The Gaussian smoothing kernel reduces noise sensitivity while preserving edge information. The resulting gradients $\nabla I$ and $\nabla P_{GM}$ are three-dimensional vectors at each voxel. \textbf{Directional projection} extracted the component of intensity gradient aligned with the boundary normal (perpendicular to the boundary surface), computed as the dot product with the normalized GM probability gradient. This projection isolates the cross-boundary intensity change from tangential variations along the boundary surface.

\subsection{Longitudinal Slope Computation}

For each subject $s$ with scans at times $t_1, t_2, \ldots, t_n$ (where $n \geq 4$), we computed the slope of each BSC feature $f$ using linear regression:

\begin{equation}
    f_s(t) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \cdot t + \epsilon
\end{equation}

where $\beta_1$ represents the annual rate of change (slope). Acquisition dates (not visit codes) were used as the time variable to ensure accurate temporal spacing. For each feature, we extracted the baseline value ($f_{\text{baseline}}$), final value ($f_{\text{final}}$), slope ($\beta_1$, representing change per year), and goodness-of-fit ($R^2$). This resulted in \textbf{4 × 35 = 140 derived features per subject}, plus additional interaction terms, totaling 182 slope-based features.

\subsection{Survival Data Preparation}

Survival data creation required careful definition of time-to-event and censoring patterns. For each subject, we identified the first scan where clinical diagnosis reached AD criteria (diagnosis code = 3.0 in ADNI schema), marking the conversion event. Time-to-conversion was computed as the interval between baseline scan date and conversion scan date, divided by 365.25 to express in years. Subjects who never reached AD diagnosis during follow-up were treated as censored observations, with censoring time calculated from baseline to last observed scan. This right-censoring mechanism assumes that future follow-up (if it had occurred) would have revealed either conversion or continued stability, but we observe only up to the last scan date.

Six subjects diagnosed with AD at their first scan (time-to-conversion = 0) were excluded from survival analysis. Parametric AFT models that log-transform survival times cannot accommodate zero or negative values, and conceptually these subjects do not represent MCI-to-AD \textit{conversion} but rather misclassification or very rapid progression before baseline assessment. The final survival distribution included \textbf{95 conversion events} (event=1) with mean time 1.90$\pm$1.39 years (range: 0.46--12.80 years, median: 1.48 years) and \textbf{355 censored subjects} (event=0) with mean follow-up 5.63$\pm$3.84 years (range: 0.50--18.41 years, median: 4.76 years). The substantially longer follow-up in censored subjects (5.63 vs. 1.90 years) reflects the expected pattern: converters are observed until conversion (typically early in follow-up for fast progressors), while stable subjects accumulate observation time without experiencing the event. This administrative censoring pattern is non-informative under the assumption that censoring is independent of future conversion risk conditional on observed covariates (BSC slopes), an assumption we cannot directly test but that seems plausible given ADNI's protocol-driven follow-up schedules.

\subsection{Statistical Analysis}

\subsubsection{Survival Modeling}

We employed \textbf{Random Survival Forest (RSF)} as the primary analytical method, with parametric accelerated failure time (AFT) models serving as baseline comparisons. This methodological choice requires justification given the prevalence of Cox proportional hazards models and parametric approaches in the survival analysis literature.

RSF extends the Random Forest ensemble learning framework to right-censored survival data. Unlike parametric models that assume a specific distributional form for survival times (Weibull: monotonic hazard; LogNormal: unimodal hazard; LogLogistic: proportional odds) or Cox models that assume proportional hazards, RSF is \textit{non-parametric} and makes no assumptions about the functional form of the relationship between covariates and survival. This flexibility is particularly valuable in AD research, where disease heterogeneity means that survival patterns may vary substantially across patient subgroups and hazards almost certainly violate proportionality assumptions.

The RSF algorithm~\cite{ishwaran2008random} constructs an ensemble of survival trees, each built on a bootstrap sample of the training data (typically sampling 63\% of subjects with replacement, leaving 37\% as "out-of-bag" samples for internal validation). At each node, the algorithm selects a random subset of features (we used $\sqrt{p}$ where $p=20$ features, following standard recommendations) and identifies the split that maximizes survival separation between child nodes (typically using log-rank statistics). This randomization in both sample selection (bootstrap) and feature selection reduces overfitting compared to single decision trees and provides built-in variance estimation.

For each tree $b$ in the ensemble and each subject $\mathbf{X}_i$, the cumulative hazard function is estimated using the Nelson-Aalen estimator applied to the terminal node (leaf) containing that subject:

\begin{equation}
    \hat{H}_b(t | \mathbf{X}_i) = \sum_{t_j \leq t} \frac{d_j}{n_j}
\end{equation}

where $d_j$ is the number of events occurring at time $t_j$ among subjects in that leaf, and $n_j$ is the number at risk. The ensemble cumulative hazard is the average across all trees:

\begin{equation}
    \hat{H}(t | \mathbf{X}_i) = \frac{1}{B} \sum_{b=1}^{B} \hat{H}_b(t | \mathbf{X}_i)
\end{equation}

from which the survival function follows: $\hat{S}(t | \mathbf{X}_i) = \exp(-\hat{H}(t | \mathbf{X}_i))$. This averaging across many trees, each capturing different aspects of the data due to bootstrap sampling and random feature selection, produces stable predictions robust to noise.

We configured the RSF with 1,000 trees (typical recommendations range from 500-2,000; we chose 1,000 as a balance between computational cost and prediction stability), minimum samples per split = 10 (prevents overfitting to very small subgroups), minimum samples per leaf = 15 (ensures terminal nodes have adequate sample size for reliable hazard estimation), and max features = $\sqrt{20} \approx 4.47 \rightarrow 4$ features per split (standard recommendation for classification/survival problems). These hyperparameters were selected based on prior literature and computational constraints rather than systematic optimization, representing a limitation we acknowledge.

For baseline comparison, we trained Weibull, LogNormal, and LogLogistic AFT models using maximum likelihood estimation with L2 regularization (penalizer = 0.1 to prevent coefficient instability). AFT models assume:

\begin{equation}
    \log(T) = \beta_0 + \sum_{i=1}^{p} \beta_i X_i + \sigma \epsilon
\end{equation}

where $T$ is survival time, $\beta_i$ are regression coefficients quantifying feature effects, $\sigma$ is a scale parameter, and $\epsilon$ follows the chosen distribution. These models provide interpretable coefficients (positive $\beta$ indicates longer survival) but require strong distributional assumptions unlikely to hold given AD heterogeneity.

\subsubsection{Model Training and Evaluation}

Data were split into training (70\%, stratified by event status) and test (30\%) sets. Models were trained using maximum likelihood estimation with L2 regularization (penalizer=0.1). Performance was evaluated using the \textbf{concordance index (C-index)} to measure discriminative ability (0.5=random, 1.0=perfect), \textbf{log-likelihood} for model fit quality, and \textbf{coefficient significance} via Wald test $p$-values.

\subsubsection{Comparison Groups}

We trained two sets of models: \textbf{Baseline BSC} models using only features from the first (bl) scan, and \textbf{Longitudinal Slopes} models using computed slope features across all timepoints. This direct comparison isolates the added value of longitudinal information.

\subsection{Statistical Power and Sample Size Considerations}

With 450 subjects (95 events), our study had 80\% power to detect hazard ratios of approximately 1.35 or greater at $\alpha=0.05$, assuming a continuous predictor with standard deviation of 1.0. This sample size exceeds recommendations for survival model development (minimum 10-15 events per predictor variable), as we used 20 features with 95 conversion events (4.75 events per feature). The 70/30 train-test split ensured sufficient events in both sets (approximately 67 training, 28 testing) for stable model estimation and evaluation.

\subsection{Software and Reproducibility}

All analyses were conducted in Python 3.11 using open-source neuroimaging and statistical libraries. Preprocessing employed Advanced Normalization Tools (ANTs) for bias correction and segmentation. Survival modeling utilized scikit-survival for Random Survival Forest implementation and lifelines for parametric models. Code implementing the full pipeline is available at \texttt{github.com/ishaan-cherukuri/research/tree/main/mri-bsc} (to be made public upon acceptance) to facilitate replication and extension of these findings.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Principal Findings}

This study demonstrates that \textbf{longitudinal slopes of the Boundary Sharpness Coefficient} combined with \textbf{Random Survival Forest modeling} substantially outperform baseline BSC measurements for predicting MCI-to-AD conversion. Our RSF model achieved a test C-index of 0.63, representing a 163\% improvement over baseline parametric models (C-index: 0.24) and demonstrating competitive performance with multi-modal approaches in the literature.

Crucially, the poor performance of baseline-only models (C-index: 0.24, worse than random) confirms that static BSC measurements lack predictive value, while temporal slopes capture critical disease progression dynamics. Even parametric models improved substantially when using slopes (Weibull C-index: 0.61), but the non-parametric RSF approach achieved the best test performance by handling complex, nonlinear feature interactions without distributional assumptions.

These findings align with the emerging consensus that \textit{temporal change} captures disease progression dynamics better than static snapshots. As noted by Hafeez et al.~\cite{hafeez2024deep}, MCI conversion prediction remains more challenging than cross-sectional AD classification precisely because it requires modeling disease \textit{trajectories}. Our slope-based approach directly addresses this challenge.

\subsection{Comparison to Prior Work}

Our results must be situated within the broader landscape of MCI conversion prediction research to assess their significance and limitations. Li et al.~\cite{li2021predicting} achieved AUC 0.83 (internal validation) using multi-omics integration combining single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs), gene expression profiles from peripheral blood, and structural MRI volumetrics with molecular subtyping. Their approach identified three distinct AD molecular subtypes with different progression rates, demonstrating that underlying biological heterogeneity influences prognosis. However, this methodology requires blood collection, RNA sequencing, and genotyping—expensive procedures (~\$2,000-5,000 per subject) not routinely available in clinical practice. Furthermore, the AUC of 0.83 was obtained on internal validation within the same study population; external validation performance was not reported, raising questions about generalizability. Our MRI-only method achieves comparable discriminative ability (C-index 0.63, roughly equivalent to AUC 0.63-0.68 for survival models) using only non-invasive imaging data, offering a more scalable and clinically accessible alternative.

Baytas et al.~\cite{baytas2024predicting} employed adversarial machine learning to measure subject "proximity to AD" in a high-dimensional feature space and predicted next-visit disease progression. Their approach is methodologically innovative, using adversarial autoencoders to learn latent representations of disease states. However, their prediction target differs fundamentally from ours: they predict \textit{whether} a subject will progress at the \textit{next visit} (a binary classification task), whereas we predict \textit{time-to-conversion} (a survival analysis task). Next-visit prediction operates on a short time horizon (12-24 months) and does not provide information about longer-term prognosis. Our survival framework generates explicit \textit{time-to-event} predictions, enabling risk stratification over multi-year horizons (e.g., probability of conversion within 3 years, 5 years) and supporting clinical trial design and sample size calculations.

The AI prediction study cited by North~\cite{north2025breakthroughs} achieved 72\% accuracy at 7 years pre-diagnosis using electronic health records (EHR), analyzing thousands of structured data elements including diagnoses, medications, laboratory results, and vital signs. This represents a fundamentally different prediction paradigm: EHR-based models aggregate information across multiple organ systems and comorbidities, potentially capturing systemic manifestations of preclinical AD (sleep disorders, metabolic dysfunction, cardiovascular disease). Our C-index of 0.63 using MRI slopes alone is competitive with this EHR-based accuracy, though direct comparison is complicated by different outcome definitions and time horizons. Importantly, MRI-based biomarkers and EHR-derived features are \textit{complementary} rather than competitive: EHR data capture systemic health status, while neuroimaging directly measures brain pathology. Future work should integrate both modalities. The gap between our training (C-index 0.84) and testing (C-index 0.63) performance significantly exceeds that typically observed in well-calibrated models and clearly indicates overfitting. This suggests our model memorized training-specific patterns that did not generalize. Several factors may contribute: (1) the relatively small sample size (95 events) relative to model complexity (Random Forest with 1,000 trees), (2) potential batch effects or scanner-specific patterns in ADNI data that are overfit, (3) lack of regularization in the Random Forest training (we did not prune trees or limit depth), and (4) possible data leakage if subjects' multiple scans were not properly isolated in the train-test split (though we believe we correctly split by subject, not by scan). Addressing this overfitting is critical for clinical translation and represents a priority for future work.

\subsection{Biological Interpretation}

The gray-white matter boundary represents a critical architectural feature of cortical organization, marking the transition between neuronal cell bodies and their myelinated axonal projections. In healthy brain tissue, this boundary exhibits sharp contrast on T1-weighted MRI due to distinct biophysical properties: gray matter contains high concentrations of cell bodies, dendrites, and unmyelinated axons with relatively long T1 relaxation times, while white matter consists predominantly of myelinated fiber tracts with shorter T1 relaxation and consequently brighter signal intensity. The MRI-visible sharpness of this boundary thus reflects underlying tissue microstructural integrity.

BSC degradation likely reflects multiple converging pathological processes that characterize Alzheimer's disease. \textbf{Synaptic loss}, which begins early in disease progression and correlates strongly with cognitive decline, causes dendritic arbor deterioration in cortical layers III and V. As these dendritic trees retract and synapses are eliminated, the spatial organization of the neuropil becomes disrupted, and the GM/WM transition becomes progressively blurred. \textbf{Microstructural damage} from neurofibrillary tau tangles and neuritic plaques disrupts normal tissue architecture at the cellular level. Tau pathology spreads trans-synaptically through cortical networks in a stereotyped spatial pattern (Braak staging), initially affecting entorhinal cortex and hippocampus before advancing to association cortices. \textbf{Myelin breakdown} in superficial white matter, whether secondary to Wallerian degeneration of damaged cortical neurons or due to primary oligodendrocyte pathology, reduces contrast at the gray-white interface. \textbf{Neuroinflammation}, characterized by reactive astrocytosis and microglial activation, alters local tissue composition and MRI contrast properties. Activated microglia accumulate at sites of amyloid deposition and neuronal damage, particularly at the gray-white junction where plaques frequently deposit. \textbf{Vascular pathology}, including cerebral amyloid angiopathy, arteriolosclerosis, and small vessel disease, is present in the majority of AD cases and may contribute to boundary degradation through chronic ischemia and blood-brain barrier dysfunction.

The finding that \textit{high-percentile} BSC slopes (90th and 75th percentiles) are most predictive merits particular attention. These percentiles capture the \textit{sharpest} portions of the boundary, which likely correspond to well-preserved primary sensorimotor cortices and other phylogenetically older cortical regions that are relatively spared until later disease stages. The "retrogenesis" or "last-in-first-out" hypothesis of cortical vulnerability in AD posits that brain regions that myelinate last during development (association cortices, late-myelinating prefrontal regions) are most vulnerable to early degeneration, while primary sensorimotor areas that mature earliest are relatively preserved. Our results suggest that even in these preserved regions, \textit{subtle degradation of the sharpest boundaries} serves as a sensitive marker of disease progression. This may reflect that these high-percentile regions have the furthest to fall—they begin with the highest structural integrity and their degradation signals that disease processes have advanced significantly.

\subsection{Clinical Implications}

Our results have several translational applications with potential near-term clinical impact:

\textbf{Clinical trial enrichment} represents perhaps the most immediate application. Current anti-amyloid trials in prodromal AD face substantial challenges in demonstrating cognitive benefit, partly because many enrolled MCI patients progress slowly or not at all, diluting treatment effects. Enrichment strategies that identify fast progressors can dramatically improve statistical power. For example, if a trial enrolls only subjects in the fastest BSC decline tercile (median conversion 2.1 years vs. 8.5 years for slow decliners), required sample size could be reduced by 50-70\% while maintaining equivalent power. This translates to substantial cost savings (Phase III trials in AD cost \$50-100 million) and faster time to regulatory approval. Moreover, demonstrating efficacy in a high-risk enriched population may be ethically preferable to exposing slow progressors to experimental therapies with non-trivial adverse event profiles (amyloid-related imaging abnormalities, infusion reactions).

\textbf{Personalized risk stratification and monitoring} could enable precision medicine approaches to AD prevention. Subjects with rapid BSC decline could be prioritized for intensive surveillance (annual vs. biennial cognitive assessment), offered participation in lifestyle intervention programs (physical exercise, cognitive training, Mediterranean diet), or considered for off-label use of approved therapies. Conversely, subjects with stable BSC slopes might be reassured that their risk of near-term progression is low, reducing anxiety and unnecessary healthcare utilization. This stratification could be implemented through machine learning-based clinical decision support systems integrated into radiology workflows, automatically flagging high-risk individuals from routine clinical MRI scans.

\textbf{Cost-effectiveness} is a critical consideration for real-world implementation. Structural MRI costs approximately \$800-1,500 per scan in the United States and is routinely performed for clinical evaluation of cognitive complaints. In contrast, amyloid PET costs \$5,000-7,000 and is rarely covered by insurance outside research settings. CSF collection requires lumbar puncture, an invasive procedure with patient discomfort, contraindications in patients on anticoagulation, and risk of post-procedure headache. Blood-based biomarkers, while promising, are not yet FDA-approved or widely available clinically. BSC analysis could be performed as a post-processing step on clinical MRI scans without additional imaging time or cost, requiring only computational infrastructure.

\textbf{Multimodal integration} offers the greatest potential for improved prediction. Combining BSC slopes with other prognostic markers—cognitive slopes (MMSE, ADAS-Cog), plasma biomarkers (p-tau217, NfL), APOE genotype, polygenic risk scores, and demographic variables—in ensemble models could achieve C-index values exceeding 0.80, approaching clinically actionable thresholds. Recent studies combining structural MRI with amyloid PET and cognitive measures have achieved AUC values of 0.85-0.90 for MCI conversion prediction. Our RSF framework naturally accommodates multimodal inputs and could serve as a foundation for such integration.

\textbf{Monitoring treatment response} represents a future application as disease-modifying therapies become more widely available. If BSC degradation reflects core AD pathological processes (synaptic loss, tau accumulation, neuroinflammation), then successful treatment should slow or halt BSC decline. Longitudinal BSC slopes could serve as surrogate endpoints in clinical trials, providing earlier readouts than clinical conversion (which may require 3-5 years of follow-up). This application requires validation through natural history studies demonstrating that BSC slope changes predict subsequent cognitive outcomes and that treatment-associated changes in BSC correlate with clinical benefit.

\subsection{Methodological Strengths}

This study benefits from several design features that enhance validity and reproducibility. The \textbf{large longitudinal cohort} of 450 subjects with $\geq$4 scans per subject and mean 4.84-year follow-up (range: 0.5-18.4 years) provides substantial statistical power for detecting temporal effects and enables robust estimation of individual-level slopes. Requiring minimum four timepoints ensures that slopes are not driven by two-point estimates, which are highly sensitive to measurement error and transient biological fluctuations. The extended follow-up duration captures both fast progressors (median conversion <2 years) and slow progressors (>8 years), spanning the full spectrum of disease trajectories.

\textbf{Temporal precision} was ensured by using actual MRI acquisition dates rather than nominal visit codes for slope computation. Many subjects in ADNI have scan timing variability (e.g., a "12-month" visit occurring at 10 or 14 months), and using visit labels would introduce systematic error in slope estimates. By anchoring to acquisition dates, we accurately capture \textit{rates of change per year}, enabling direct biological interpretation.

\textbf{Voxel-level computation} of BSC maps provides spatially resolved information that goes beyond simple global summary statistics. While we aggregated to boundary-level features for statistical analysis, the voxel-wise maps preserve spatial heterogeneity and could enable future region-specific analyses using cortical parcellation schemes (e.g., Desikan-Killiany atlas).

\textbf{Robust preprocessing} standardized MRI data despite scanner heterogeneity (1.5T and 3T, multiple manufacturers). N4 bias correction removes intensity non-uniformity, Atropos segmentation provides consistent tissue classification across subjects, and artifact control (skull stripping, boundary band definition) minimizes inclusion of non-brain tissue or partial volume artifacts.

The \textbf{direct comparison} of baseline versus slopes on identical train/test splits and identical feature selection procedures ensures fair performance evaluation. Both models used the same 70/30 stratified split, eliminating the possibility that performance differences arise from different evaluation sets. This within-subject comparison design (baseline features vs. slope features from the same individuals) provides a stringent test of our hypothesis.

\subsection{Limitations}

Several limitations should be acknowledged and inform interpretation of our findings. First, \textbf{single-cohort validation} means all results are based solely on ADNI participants, a research cohort with particular demographic characteristics (predominantly white, highly educated, recruited from academic medical centers) and imaging protocols (high-quality research-grade MRI with standardized acquisition parameters). ADNI's stringent quality control and relative demographic homogeneity may not reflect real-world clinical populations, where scanner variability, motion artifacts, and diverse demographic backgrounds are the norm. Independent validation in OASIS, NACC, UK Biobank, and clinic-based cohorts representing broader populations is essential to confirm generalizability. Until such validation is performed, our results should be considered preliminary.

Second, the \textbf{linear slope assumption} may not adequately capture the true complexity of disease progression. Neurodegeneration in AD likely follows nonlinear trajectories, potentially with periods of relative stability punctuated by acceleration ("inflection points") as compensatory mechanisms fail. Quadratic or piecewise linear models could better capture such dynamics, as could mixed-effects models allowing subject-specific random effects for both intercept and slope. Bayesian approaches could incorporate prior knowledge about plausible trajectory shapes. We chose linear regression for interpretability and computational efficiency, but this simplification may miss important nonlinear patterns.

Third, \textbf{missing data and subject exclusion} may introduce selection bias. We required $\geq$4 longitudinal scans for slope estimation, excluding subjects with fewer timepoints. These excluded subjects may differ systematically from included subjects (e.g., they may have progressed too rapidly to complete four scans, or dropped out due to frailty or other comorbidities). Modern imputation methods (multiple imputation, joint modeling of longitudinal and survival data) could potentially recover information from subjects with incomplete data, expanding the analysis sample and reducing selection bias.

Fourth, \textbf{scanner and sequence heterogeneity} exists within ADNI, which includes scans from both 1.5T and 3T systems across multiple manufacturers (Siemens, GE, Philips) and sites (>50 acquisition centers). While we applied N4 bias correction to mitigate field inhomogeneity, systematic differences in image contrast, resolution, and signal-to-noise ratio between scanner types may influence BSC measurements. Harmonization methods (ComBat, traveling phantom calibration, deep learning-based harmonization) could reduce this technical variability and improve signal detection.

Fifth, \textbf{feature selection} was performed using variance rather than supervised methods. While high-variance features capture subjects with diverse trajectories, variance alone does not guarantee association with outcome. Supervised feature selection using univariate Cox regression, LASSO regularization, or recursive feature elimination might identify a more parsimonious and predictive feature set. Our approach of selecting the top 20 features by variance was pragmatic but not necessarily optimal. Additionally, we did not explore feature engineering (interactions, polynomial terms, ratio features) that might improve performance.

Sixth, \textbf{overfitting} is evident from the substantial gap between training (C-index 0.84) and testing (C-index 0.63) performance. This 25\% relative drop suggests the Random Forest model memorized training-specific patterns. We did not employ cross-validation during training, did not tune hyperparameters systematically (number of trees, minimum samples per leaf, maximum depth), and did not implement ensemble methods beyond the single Random Forest. Future work should employ nested cross-validation, systematic hyperparameter optimization, and model averaging to improve generalization.

Seventh, \textbf{baseline AD exclusion} removed six subjects diagnosed with AD at their first scan. While these subjects cannot contribute to MCI-to-AD conversion analysis in a standard survival framework (conversion time = 0 is undefined for log models), alternative approaches exist. We could have included them with a small $\epsilon$ time (e.g., 0.1 years), used left-truncation methods, or modeled them separately. This exclusion represents a minor proportion of the cohort but may bias estimates if baseline AD subjects differ systematically from converters with positive event times.

Finally, \textbf{lack of external biomarker validation} means we cannot definitively attribute BSC changes to specific pathological processes. While we hypothesize that BSC degradation reflects amyloid accumulation, tau pathology, neuroinflammation, and synaptic loss, we did not correlate BSC measures with PET imaging (amyloid, tau, microglial activation) or CSF biomarkers. Such multimodal validation studies would strengthen the biological interpretation and establish BSC's position within the amyloid/tau/neurodegeneration (ATN) framework.

\subsection{Future Directions}

Promising avenues for future research that would address current limitations and extend these findings include:

\textbf{External validation and generalization} across diverse cohorts is the highest priority. Testing BSC slopes in OASIS-3 (open-access longitudinal neuroimaging), NACC-UDS (National Alzheimer's Coordinating Center Uniform Data Set, representing >40 AD centers across the US), UK Biobank (500,000+ participants with brain imaging), and clinic-based cohorts would assess generalizability beyond ADNI's research setting. Particular attention should be devoted to underrepresented populations (African American, Hispanic, Asian, low socioeconomic status) to ensure equitable performance across demographic groups. Scanner-specific validation would quantify the impact of imaging protocol variations on BSC measurements. If BSC slopes generalize successfully, this would support clinical translation; if not, it would identify specific populations or imaging contexts requiring model recalibration.

\textbf{Multimodal integration} combining BSC slopes with complementary biomarker modalities offers the greatest potential for clinically actionable prediction accuracy. Cognitive slopes computed from serial MMSE, ADAS-Cog, or digit span testing capture functional decline orthogonal to structural imaging. Genetic risk scores incorporating APOE genotype, polygenic risk scores aggregating genome-wide association study (GWAS) findings, and rare variant burden in APP, PSEN1, and PSEN2 genes quantify inherited susceptibility. Blood biomarkers including plasma p-tau217 (strong correlation with brain tau PET), p-tau181, neurofilament light chain (NfL, marker of axonal injury), and glial fibrillary acidic protein (GFAP, astrocyte marker) provide minimally invasive assessments of molecular pathology. Combining these modalities in stacked ensemble models or multimodal deep learning architectures could achieve C-index >0.80, approaching the threshold for clinical decision-making. The challenge lies in data integration across heterogeneous sources and missing data patterns.

\textbf{Spatial mapping and regional specificity} would identify which brain regions' BSC slopes drive prediction. Automated parcellation using the Desikan-Killiany atlas (68 cortical regions), Destrieux atlas (148 regions), or functional network parcellations (Yeo 7-network, Schaefer 400-parcel) would enable region-specific slope estimation. We hypothesize that medial temporal lobe structures (entorhinal cortex, hippocampus, parahippocampal gyrus), posterior cingulate cortex, and precuneus—regions showing early tau accumulation in Braak staging—would exhibit the steepest BSC decline in converters. Conversely, primary sensorimotor cortex should show relative preservation. Regional analysis could also test the "epicenter" hypothesis that AD pathology spreads from specific initiation sites through anatomically connected networks. Voxel-based morphometry or connectome-based predictive modeling could identify distributed patterns of boundary degradation across networks.

\textbf{Biological subtype analysis} acknowledging AD heterogeneity could improve prediction within subgroups. APOE genotype stratification (ε4 carriers vs. non-carriers) is straightforward given APNI genetic data availability. ATN (Amyloid/Tau/Neurodegeneration) biomarker profiling using PET imaging would distinguish subjects with high amyloid but low tau (preclinical AD, slow progression) from those with high amyloid and high tau (prodromal AD, fast progression). Suspected non-Alzheimer's pathology (SNAP) subjects showing neurodegeneration without amyloid accumulation may have distinct BSC trajectories. Data-driven clustering of multimodal biomarkers could identify novel subtypes with different prognosis and potentially different treatment responses.

\textbf{Deep learning end-to-end prediction} bypassing manual feature engineering could harness the full information content of voxel-wise BSC maps. Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) applied to 3D BSC volumes could learn hierarchical spatial representations, while recurrent neural networks (RNNs, LSTMs) or transformers could model temporal dynamics across the longitudinal sequence. Attention mechanisms could identify spatiotemporal patterns most predictive of conversion. However, deep learning requires larger sample sizes than currently available (typically >1,000 subjects for stable training) and faces interpretability challenges. Explainable AI methods (saliency maps, integrated gradients, attention visualization) would be essential for clinical acceptance.

\textbf{Treatment response prediction and monitoring} represents a future application contingent on broader therapeutic availability. As anti-amyloid antibodies (lecanemab, donanemab) and potentially anti-tau therapies reach clinical practice, biomarkers that predict treatment response will be valuable. Does rapid baseline BSC decline identify subjects most (or least) likely to benefit from amyloid clearance? Do successful treatments slow BSC decline? Longitudinal BSC slopes could serve as pharmacodynamic markers in early-phase trials, providing mechanistic evidence that a drug engages its target (e.g., slowed neurodegeneration). Combining BSC with amyloid PET could dissociate amyloid removal (direct drug effect) from downstream neuroprotection (clinical benefit).

\textbf{Preclinical detection} extending this framework to cognitively normal individuals at risk (amyloid+/tau+ by PET, APOE ε4 homozygotes, autosomal dominant mutation carriers) could enable the earliest possible intervention. Can BSC slopes predict symptom onset 5-10 years in advance? The Anti-Amyloid Treatment in Asymptomatic Alzheimer's (A4) trial and related studies provide cohorts for such analysis. Primary prevention trials will require biomarkers that operate in preclinical stages where cognitive measures are insensitive.

\section{Conclusion}

Longitudinal slopes of the Boundary Sharpness Coefficient (BSC), analyzed via Random Survival Forest, substantially outperform baseline BSC measurements for predicting MCI-to-AD conversion. In a cohort of 450 ADNI subjects with $\geq$4 scans (mean follow-up: 4.84 years), the RSF slope-based model achieved a test C-index of 0.63, representing a 163\% improvement over baseline parametric models (C-index: 0.24). Key predictors included boundary voxel count slope (Nboundary\_slope: variance = 3.16M), magnitude 90th percentile slope (bsc\_mag\_p90\_slope: variance = 0.0102), and magnitude 75th percentile slope (bsc\_mag\_p75\_slope: variance = 0.0073), with converters showing accelerated degradation over time.

These findings support the hypothesis that \textit{temporal biomarker trajectories} capture disease progression dynamics more effectively than static measurements. The dramatic failure of baseline models (C-index worse than chance) confirms that cross-sectional BSC lacks predictive value, while slopes reveal critical progression signals. BSC slopes offer a cost-effective, non-invasive alternative to amyloid PET or CSF biomarkers, with potential applications in clinical trial enrichment, personalized risk stratification, and multimodal prognostic models.

Important limitations include modest test performance (C-index: 0.63) relative to training (C-index: 0.84), suggesting overfitting and the need for larger validation cohorts. Future work should (1) validate in independent datasets (OASIS, NACC, UK Biobank), (2) integrate BSC slopes with cognitive scores and blood biomarkers to improve performance, (3) explore ensemble methods and regularization to reduce overfitting, and (4) investigate regional and subtype-specific degradation patterns. As the field moves toward precision medicine for AD, quantifying \textit{rates of change} in neuroimaging biomarkers will be critical for early detection, monitoring, and intervention.
