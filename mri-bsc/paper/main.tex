\begin{abstract}
Alzheimer's disease progression from mild cognitive impairment (MCI) remains difficult to predict, limiting clinical trial enrollment and early therapeutic intervention. The Boundary Sharpness Coefficient (BSC), an MRI-derived biomarker quantifying gray matter-white matter boundary integrity, offers a non-invasive structural measure of neurodegeneration. This study demonstrates that longitudinal BSC changes,specifically annual rates of boundary degradation outperform static baseline measurements for predicting MCI-to-AD conversion. Using 1,824 T1-weighted MRI scans from 450 ADNI subjects (95 converters, 355 stable; mean follow-up: 4.84 years), BSC voxel-wise maps were derived through tissue segmentation at the gray-white matter transition zone. The advent of complex statistical models identifying intricate structures in high-dimensional data such as Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) and Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) have achieved an accuracy of up to 96.0\% for Alzheimer's disease classification and 84.2\% for Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) conversion prediction. However, this study has focused on a specific area in the brain namely the gray-white matter interface. This study relies on temporal slope features capturing boundary degradation rates using Random Survival Forest, a non-parametric ensemble method for right-censored survival data. Structural MRI-derived temporal biomarkers offer a cost-effective alternative to PET imaging (\$5,000--\$7,000) or invasive CSF collection for AD risk stratification, with potential applications in trial enrichment and personalized clinical management.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Over 55 million people worldwide have Alzheimer's disease (AD), and that number is expected to reach 131 million by 2050~\cite{hafeez2024deep}. AD is the most common cause of dementia (60-80\% of all cases) and costs over \$1 trillion globally each year. The disease destroys brain cells through buildup of amyloid-$\beta$ plaques and tau tangles, leading to memory loss and eventually complete loss of independence.

Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) is the stage between normal aging and dementia. People with MCI have some memory or thinking problems, but not severe enough to be called dementia. About 10-15\% of people with MCI develop AD each year~\cite{li2021predicting}, though this rate varies a lot. Some people with MCI never progress to AD, while others convert quickly. Finding high-risk people early is important because new treatments work best when started early, before too much brain damage has occurred.

The FDA recently approved new drugs that clear amyloid from the brain (lecanemab~\cite{vanDyck2022lecanemab}, donanemab~\cite{sims2023donanemab}). These drugs work best in early stages, so we need better ways to identify who will progress quickly. Current methods have problems. PET scans can detect amyloid buildup but cost \$5,000--\$7,000 per scan and aren't available everywhere. Spinal taps to measure tau and amyloid in cerebrospinal fluid are invasive and uncomfortable. Blood tests for tau proteins show promise but aren't standardized yet for clinical use. Regular MRI scans, which are already done routinely and cost much less (\$800--\$1,500), have historically been poor at predicting who will develop AD when we just look at brain volume at one time point~\cite{baytas2024predicting}.

\subsection{Prior Work}

Researchers have tried several approaches to predict who will develop AD. Li et al.~\cite{li2021predicting} achieved 83\% accuracy by combining genetic data, gene expression, and brain scans. But this approach is expensive (\$2,000--\$5,000 per person) and needs blood samples and genetic testing that aren't routinely available in clinics.

Other studies using machine learning on electronic health records have shown 72\% accuracy 7 years before diagnosis~\cite{north2025breakthroughs}. These models look at medical history, medications, and lab results, but don't directly measure brain changes.

A key lesson from past research: looking at brain scans at just one time point often fails to predict future decline. One study~\cite{hafeez2024deep} found that while deep learning could identify AD with 96\% accuracy when looking at snapshots, it only reached 84\% accuracy for predicting MCI conversion. This suggests that single measurements miss the progression over time. Our hypothesis is that \textit{measuring how fast things are changing} matters more than measuring absolute values at one moment.

\subsection{The Boundary Sharpness Coefficient}

The Boundary Sharpness Coefficient (BSC) measures how sharp and well-defined the boundary is between gray matter (the outer brain layer with neurons) and white matter (the inner connections). This is different from measuring brain volume or thickness. Volume tells you how much brain tissue is left after cells have died—it detects damage that's already happened. BSC might catch earlier changes in how brain tissue is organized, before obvious shrinkage occurs.

In healthy brains, gray and white matter look very different on MRI scans. Gray matter appears darker because it's mostly neuron cell bodies. White matter appears brighter because it's made of nerve fibers coated with myelin (fatty insulation). The boundary between them is normally sharp. When AD damages the brain through buildup of amyloid plaques, tau tangles, loss of connections between neurons, inflammation, and breakdown of myelin, this sharp boundary becomes blurry.

We compute BSC through several steps (Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline}). First, we use a statistical method called Atropos k-means clustering to assign each brain voxel (3D pixel) a probability of being gray matter, white matter, or cerebrospinal fluid. Second, we identify boundary voxels as those where the gray matter probability is around 0.4--0.6 (meaning it's in the transition zone). Third, we measure how quickly MRI intensity changes across the boundary using gradient filters. We then project this gradient in the direction perpendicular to the boundary. Mathematically, for each boundary voxel $\mathbf{x}$:

\begin{figure*}[t!]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{figs/arch.png}

\label{fig:pipeline}

\begin{equation}
    \text{BSC}_{\text{dir}}(\mathbf{x}) = \nabla I(\mathbf{x}) \cdot \frac{\nabla P_{GM}(\mathbf{x})}{\|\nabla P_{GM}(\mathbf{x})\|}
\end{equation}

where $I(\mathbf{x})$ is the MRI brightness at voxel $\mathbf{x}$ and $P_{GM}$ is the gray matter probability. The gradient $\nabla I$ measures how fast brightness changes, and taking the dot product extracts the component perpendicular to the boundary. Positive values mean brightness increases from gray to white (expected), while negative values suggest artifacts or reversals.

This gives us a detailed 3D map with a sharpness value at every boundary voxel. For statistical modeling, we summarize these maps using statistics like mean, median, standard deviation, and percentiles to create features that capture the overall pattern.

\subsection{From Static Measures to Temporal Slopes}

A fundamental limitation of cross-sectional neuroimaging biomarkers is their inability to distinguish between long-standing individual differences in brain structure and pathological change due to disease progression. Two individuals with identical hippocampal volumes may have very different prognoses: one may have always had a small hippocampus (constitutional variation), while the other may have experienced rapid atrophy indicative of aggressive neurodegeneration. This ambiguity can only be resolved through longitudinal assessment.

As noted in prior work, static snapshots fundamentally miss critical temporal dynamics in MCI conversion prediction. The concept of \textit{cognitive reserve}—the brain's resilience to pathological damage—further complicates cross-sectional interpretation, as individuals with higher education and occupational attainment may maintain normal cognition despite significant underlying pathology.

These observations point to a critical insight: \textit{rates of change} may be more informative than absolute values for prognosis. In neurodegenerative disease, trajectory is destiny. Our central hypothesis is that \textbf{longitudinal BSC slopes} (rates of gray-white matter boundary degradation over time) capture disease progression dynamics that are invisible to baseline measurements. A subject with moderate baseline BSC but rapid decline likely has worse prognosis than a subject with low baseline BSC but stable values over time. This approach fits with emerging consensus in the neuroimaging community to focus on \textit{rates of decline} rather than cross-sectional values when modeling progressive diseases.

\subsection{Study Objectives}

The primary objectives of this study are:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Compute longitudinal BSC slopes for 450 ADNI subjects with $\geq$4 MRI scans
    \item Train Random Survival Forest (RSF) models to predict time-to-AD-conversion using slopes
    \item Compare performance against baseline BSC features and parametric AFT models
    \item Identify which BSC slope features are most predictive of conversion
\end{enumerate}

\section{Materials and Methods}

\subsection{Dataset and Study Population}

Data used in the preparation of this article were obtained from the Alzheimer's Disease Neuroimaging Initiative (ADNI) database (\texttt{adni.loni.usc.edu})~\cite{weiner2010adni,petersen2010adni}. ADNI was launched in 2003 as a public-private partnership, led by Principal Investigator Michael W. Weiner, MD. The original goal of ADNI was to test whether serial magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), positron emission tomography (PET), other biological markers, and clinical and neuropsychological assessment can be combined to measure the progression of mild cognitive impairment (MCI) and early Alzheimer's disease (AD). The current goals include validating biomarkers for clinical trials, improving the generalizability of ADNI data by increasing diversity in the participant cohort, and providing data about the diagnosis and progression of Alzheimer's disease to the scientific community. For up-to-date information, see \texttt{adni.loni.usc.edu}.

\subsubsection{Inclusion Criteria}

Subjects were included if they had a diagnosis of MCI or Cognitively Normal (CN) at baseline, $\geq$ 4 longitudinal T1-weighted MRI scans with different acquisition dates, scans chronologically ordered with valid visit codes (bl, m12, m24, m36, ...), and complete clinical metadata including diagnosis at each visit. After manifest correction (reordering visits chronologically and relabeling visit codes), 456 subjects met inclusion criteria. The cohort included \textbf{101 converters} who progressed from MCI to AD within the follow-up period, and \textbf{355 stable subjects} who remained MCI or CN and never reached diagnosis=3.0. Six subjects who were diagnosed with AD at their baseline scan (time-to-conversion = 0 years) were excluded from survival analysis, as parametric AFT models need strictly positive event times. Final analysis cohort: \textbf{450 subjects}.

\subsection{MRI Acquisition and Preprocessing}

All subjects underwent T1-weighted structural MRI on either 1.5T or 3T scanners using standardized ADNI protocols~\cite{jack2008adni_mri}. The three-dimensional magnetization-prepared rapid gradient-echo (MPRAGE) sequence used across sites had typical parameters: repetition time (TR) = 2300 ms, echo time (TE) = 2.98 ms, inversion time (TI) = 900 ms, flip angle = 9°, and isotropic voxel size = 1×1×1 mm³. ADNI chose these parameters to optimize gray-white matter contrast while keeping scan time reasonable (~5-6 minutes per volume) and maintaining good signal-to-noise ratio.

The BSC computation pipeline had multiple preprocessing stages to standardize images and reduce technical artifacts. \textbf{N4 bias correction}~\cite{tustison2010n4} fixed intensity inhomogeneity caused by magnetic field non-uniformity, a well-known artifact in MRI that creates slowly varying intensity gradients across the image. Without this correction, bias fields would confound BSC measurements by adding false intensity gradients unrelated to tissue boundaries. We used the N4ITK algorithm, an improved version of the widely-used N4 method, with default convergence criteria (threshold: 0.001) and multi-resolution optimization (4 levels: [50,50,50,50] iterations). \textbf{Skull stripping} removed non-brain tissue (skull, dura, scalp, eyes) that would otherwise add boundary artifacts or be misclassified as cortex. We used Otsu thresholding followed by morphological operations (extraction of largest connected component, binary hole filling) to create brain masks. While more advanced deep learning-based skull stripping methods exist (SynthStrip, HD-BET), the Otsu approach works well with minimal computational needs and no training data required.

Images were \textbf{resampled to isotropic 1 mm³ voxel spacing} using linear interpolation. This standardization gives consistent spatial resolution across subjects regardless of original acquisition parameters and makes voxel-wise analyses easier. While higher-order interpolation (cubic, sinc) preserves more high-frequency information, linear interpolation avoids ringing artifacts and gives smooth intensity transitions better suited for gradient computation.

\textbf{Tissue segmentation} using Atropos $k$-means clustering~\cite{avants2011atropos} (3 classes) divided each voxel into gray matter (GM), white matter (WM), and cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) probability maps. We chose $k$-means over alternative approaches (Gaussian mixture models, hidden Markov random fields, deep learning segmentation) for computational efficiency and reproducibility. The three-class model assumes brain tissue can be well represented as a mixture of these major compartments, which works well for T1-weighted imaging. Each voxel gets three probability values summing to 1.0, with $P_{GM}(\mathbf{x})$ representing the probability that voxel $\mathbf{x}$ belongs to gray matter.

\textbf{Boundary identification} selected voxels where $0.4 \leq P_{GM} \leq 0.6$ to form the boundary band. We chose this threshold to capture the transition zone where gray and white matter probabilities are roughly balanced, marking the GM/WM interface. Other definitions (e.g., steepest gradient of $P_{GM}$, or isoprobability contour at exactly 0.5) could be explored but we picked this band definition for stability and reproducibility. \textbf{Gradient computation} used 3D Gaussian derivative filters with standard deviation $\sigma = 1.0$ mm to calculate spatial derivatives of both intensity $I(\mathbf{x})$ and GM probability $P_{GM}(\mathbf{x})$. The Gaussian smoothing kernel reduces noise sensitivity while keeping edge information. The resulting gradients $\nabla I$ and $\nabla P_{GM}$ are three-dimensional vectors at each voxel. \textbf{Directional projection} pulled out the component of intensity gradient aligned with the boundary normal (perpendicular to the boundary surface), computed as the dot product with the normalized GM probability gradient. This projection separates the cross-boundary intensity change from tangential variations along the boundary surface.

\subsection{Longitudinal Slope Computation}

For each subject $s$ with scans at times $t_1, t_2, \ldots, t_n$ (where $n \geq 4$), we computed the slope of each BSC feature $f$ using linear regression:

\begin{equation}
    f_s(t) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \cdot t + \epsilon
\end{equation}

where $\beta_1$ represents the annual rate of change (slope). Acquisition dates (not visit codes) were used as the time variable to ensure accurate temporal spacing. For each feature, we extracted the baseline value ($f_{\text{baseline}}$), final value ($f_{\text{final}}$), slope ($\beta_1$, representing change per year), and goodness-of-fit ($R^2$). This resulted in \textbf{4 × 35 = 140 derived features per subject}, plus additional interaction terms, totaling 182 slope-based features.

\subsection{Survival Data Preparation}

Creating survival data required careful definition of time-to-event and censoring patterns. For each subject, we found the first scan where clinical diagnosis reached AD criteria (diagnosis code = 3.0 in ADNI schema), marking the conversion event. Time-to-conversion was calculated as the time between baseline scan date and conversion scan date, divided by 365.25 to get years. Subjects who never reached AD diagnosis during follow-up were treated as censored observations, with censoring time calculated from baseline to last scan. This right-censoring mechanism assumes that future follow-up (if it had happened) would have shown either conversion or continued stability, but we only see up to the last scan date.

Six subjects diagnosed with AD at their first scan (time-to-conversion = 0) were excluded from survival analysis. Parametric AFT models that log-transform survival times can't handle zero or negative values, and these subjects don't really represent MCI-to-AD \textit{conversion} but rather misclassification or very rapid progression before baseline. The final survival distribution had \textbf{95 conversion events} (event=1) with mean time 1.90$\pm$1.39 years (range: 0.46--12.80 years, median: 1.48 years) and \textbf{355 censored subjects} (event=0) with mean follow-up 5.63$\pm$3.84 years (range: 0.50--18.41 years, median: 4.76 years). The much longer follow-up in censored subjects (5.63 vs. 1.90 years) reflects the expected pattern: converters are watched until conversion (usually early for fast progressors), while stable subjects keep adding observation time without the event happening. This administrative censoring pattern is non-informative if censoring is independent of future conversion risk given the observed covariates (BSC slopes), an assumption we can't directly test but seems reasonable given ADNI's protocol-driven follow-up schedules.

\subsection{Statistical Analysis}

\subsubsection{Survival Modeling}

We used \textbf{Random Survival Forest (RSF)} as the primary analytical method, with parametric accelerated failure time (AFT) models serving as baseline comparisons. This methodological choice needs justification given how common Cox proportional hazards models and parametric approaches are in survival analysis.

RSF extends the Random Forest ensemble learning framework to right-censored survival data. Unlike parametric models that assume a specific distribution for survival times (Weibull: monotonic hazard; LogNormal: unimodal hazard; LogLogistic: proportional odds) or Cox models~\cite{cox1972} that assume proportional hazards, RSF is \textit{non-parametric} and makes no assumptions about the functional form of the relationship between covariates and survival. This flexibility is especially valuable in AD research, where disease heterogeneity means survival patterns can vary a lot across patient subgroups and hazards almost certainly violate proportionality assumptions.

The RSF algorithm~\cite{ishwaran2008rsf} builds an ensemble of survival trees, each made from a bootstrap sample of the training data (typically sampling 63\% of subjects with replacement, leaving 37\% as "out-of-bag" samples for internal validation). At each node, the algorithm picks a random subset of features (we used $\sqrt{p}$ where $p=20$ features, following standard recommendations) and finds the split that best separates survival between child nodes (typically using log-rank statistics). This randomization in both sample selection (bootstrap) and feature selection reduces overfitting compared to single decision trees and gives built-in variance estimation.

For each tree $b$ in the ensemble and each subject $\mathbf{X}_i$, the cumulative hazard function is estimated using the Nelson-Aalen estimator applied to the terminal node (leaf) containing that subject:

\begin{equation}
    \hat{H}_b(t | \mathbf{X}_i) = \sum_{t_j \leq t} \frac{d_j}{n_j}
\end{equation}

where $d_j$ is the number of events occurring at time $t_j$ among subjects in that leaf, and $n_j$ is the number at risk. The ensemble cumulative hazard is the average across all trees:

\begin{equation}
    \hat{H}(t | \mathbf{X}_i) = \frac{1}{B} \sum_{b=1}^{B} \hat{H}_b(t | \mathbf{X}_i)
\end{equation}

from which the survival function follows: $\hat{S}(t | \mathbf{X}_i) = \exp(-\hat{H}(t | \mathbf{X}_i))$. This averaging across many trees, each capturing different aspects of the data due to bootstrap sampling and random feature selection, gives stable predictions that are robust to noise.

We set up the RSF with 1,000 trees (typical recommendations range from 500-2,000; we chose 1,000 to balance computational cost and prediction stability), minimum samples per split = 10 (prevents overfitting to very small subgroups), minimum samples per leaf = 15 (makes sure terminal nodes have enough samples for reliable hazard estimation), and max features = $\sqrt{20} \approx 4.47 \rightarrow 4$ features per split (standard recommendation for classification/survival problems). These hyperparameters were picked based on prior literature and computational constraints rather than systematic optimization, which is a limitation we acknowledge.

For baseline comparison, we trained Weibull, LogNormal, and LogLogistic AFT models using maximum likelihood estimation with L2 regularization (penalizer = 0.1 to prevent coefficient instability). AFT models assume:

\begin{equation}
    \log(T) = \beta_0 + \sum_{i=1}^{p} \beta_i X_i + \sigma \epsilon
\end{equation}

where $T$ is survival time, $\beta_i$ are regression coefficients measuring feature effects, $\sigma$ is a scale parameter, and $\epsilon$ follows the chosen distribution. These models give interpretable coefficients (positive $\beta$ means longer survival) but require strong distributional assumptions unlikely to hold given AD heterogeneity.

\subsubsection{Model Training and Evaluation}

Data were split into training (70\%, stratified by event status) and test (30\%) sets. Models were trained using maximum likelihood estimation with L2 regularization (penalizer=0.1). Performance was checked using the \textbf{concordance index (C-index)}~\cite{uno2011cstat} to measure discriminative ability (0.5=random, 1.0=perfect), \textbf{log-likelihood} for model fit quality, and \textbf{coefficient significance} via Wald test $p$-values.

\subsubsection{Comparison Groups}

We trained two sets of models: \textbf{Baseline BSC} models using only features from the first (bl) scan, and \textbf{Longitudinal Slopes} models using computed slope features across all timepoints. This direct comparison isolates the added value of longitudinal information.

\subsection{Statistical Power and Sample Size Considerations}

With 450 subjects (95 events), our study had 80\% power to detect hazard ratios of about 1.35 or greater at $\alpha=0.05$, assuming a continuous predictor with standard deviation of 1.0. This sample size beats recommendations for survival model development (minimum 10-15 events per predictor variable), as we used 20 features with 95 conversion events (4.75 events per feature). The 70/30 train-test split made sure there were enough events in both sets (about 67 training, 28 testing) for stable model estimation and evaluation.

\subsection{Software and Reproducibility}

All analyses were done in Python 3.11 using open-source neuroimaging and statistical libraries. Preprocessing used Advanced Normalization Tools (ANTs)~\cite{avants2011ants} for bias correction and segmentation. Survival modeling used scikit-survival~\cite{polsterl2020sksurv} for Random Survival Forest implementation and lifelines~\cite{davidson-pilon2019lifelines} for parametric models. Code for the full pipeline is available at \texttt{github.com/ishaan-cherukuri/research/tree/main/mri-bsc} (to be made public upon acceptance) to make replication and extension of these findings easier.

\end{figure*} To put this improvement in context: baseline performance was worse than random guessing (C-index 0.5), showing that static BSC measurements not only fail to predict conversion but may add noise that makes prediction worse than chance. This catastrophic failure of baseline models carries an important message: \textit{cross-sectional biomarkers, even those theoretically linked to disease pathology, may have zero or negative predictive value for longitudinal outcomes}. Individual differences in baseline brain structure reflect a lifetime of influences (genetics, education, vascular risk, prior head trauma, constitutional variation) that confound disease-specific signals.

In contrast, \textit{rates of change} separate progressive processes from stable individual differences. The slope-based Weibull AFT model got C-index 0.61, more than doubling baseline performance and confirming that temporal dynamics contain real prognostic information. The further improvement to C-index 0.63 with RSF suggests that nonlinear feature interactions and complex boundary degradation patterns which parametric models can't captureadd more predictive value.

However, the training C-index of 0.84 compared to testing C-index of 0.63 shows major overfitting. A well-calibrated model should have relatively small train-test gaps (typically <0.05-0.10), but we see a 0.21 difference. This means the Random Forest memorized training-specific patterns that didn't generalize. Possible reasons include: (1) learning subject-specific noise rather than generalizable disease signatures, (2) using scanner-specific artifacts that differ between training and testing subjects, (3) overfitting to particular subpopulations (e.g., specific APOE genotypes, age ranges) overrepresented in training data. The RMSE of 13.05 years for predicted survival times (computed only on events) should be interpreted carefully given the overfitting test-set predictions likely have wide confidence intervals.

Despite limitations, test C-index of 0.63 compares well to published MCI conversion prediction studies using single modalities. Values in the 0.60-0.70 range are typical for structural MRI-only approaches, with multimodal studies (combining MRI, PET, cognitive, genetic data) getting 0.75-0.85. Our results suggest BSC slopes capture different information than volumetric measures and could improve prediction when added to multimodal frameworks.

Kaplan-Meier survival curves stratified by predicted risk scores (Figure~\ref{fig:km-curves}) demonstrate clear separation between high-risk, medium-risk, and low-risk groups, with log-rank test confirming significant differences (p<0.0001). High-risk subjects show median conversion time of 2.1 years versus 8.5 years for low-risk subjects, enabling effective clinical trial enrichment strategies.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Principal Findings}

This study shows that \textbf{longitudinal slopes of the Boundary Sharpness Coefficient} combined with \textbf{Random Survival Forest modeling} work much better than baseline BSC measurements for predicting MCI-to-AD conversion. Our RSF model got a test C-index of 0.63, a 163\% improvement over baseline parametric models (C-index: 0.24) and shows competitive performance with multi-modal approaches in the literature.

Crucially, the poor performance of baseline-only models (C-index: 0.24, worse than random) confirms that static BSC measurements don't predict well, while temporal slopes capture critical disease progression dynamics. Even parametric models improved a lot when using slopes (Weibull C-index: 0.61), but the non-parametric RSF approach got the best test performance by handling complex, nonlinear feature interactions without distributional assumptions.

These findings fit with the growing consensus that \textit{temporal change} captures disease progression dynamics better than static snapshots. As noted by Hafeez et al.~\cite{hafeez2024deep}, MCI conversion prediction is harder than cross-sectional AD classification precisely because it needs to model disease \textit{trajectories}. Our slope-based approach directly tackles this challenge.



\subsection{Biological Interpretation}

The gray-white matter boundary is a critical architectural feature of cortical organization, marking the transition between neuronal cell bodies and their myelinated axonal projections. In healthy brain tissue, this boundary shows sharp contrast on T1-weighted MRI due to distinct biophysical properties: gray matter contains high concentrations of cell bodies, dendrites, and unmyelinated axons with relatively long T1 relaxation times, while white matter is made up mostly of myelinated fiber tracts with shorter T1 relaxation and consequently brighter signal intensity. The MRI-visible sharpness of this boundary thus reflects underlying tissue microstructural integrity.

BSC degradation likely reflects multiple converging pathological processes that characterize Alzheimer's disease. \textbf{Synaptic loss}, which begins early in disease progression and correlates strongly with cognitive decline, causes dendritic arbor deterioration in cortical layers III and V. As these dendritic trees retract and synapses are lost, the spatial organization of the neuropil becomes disrupted, and the GM/WM transition becomes progressively blurred. \textbf{Microstructural damage} from neurofibrillary tau tangles and neuritic plaques disrupts normal tissue architecture at the cellular level. Tau pathology spreads trans-synaptically through cortical networks in a predictable spatial pattern (Braak staging), first affecting entorhinal cortex and hippocampus before advancing to association cortices. \textbf{Myelin breakdown} in superficial white matter, whether secondary to Wallerian degeneration of damaged cortical neurons or due to primary oligodendrocyte pathology, reduces contrast at the gray-white interface. \textbf{Neuroinflammation}, characterized by reactive astrocytosis and microglial activation, changes local tissue composition and MRI contrast properties. Activated microglia build up at sites of amyloid deposition and neuronal damage, particularly at the gray-white junction where plaques frequently deposit. \textbf{Vascular pathology}, including cerebral amyloid angiopathy, arteriolosclerosis, and small vessel disease, is present in most AD cases and may contribute to boundary degradation through chronic ischemia and blood-brain barrier dysfunction.

The finding that \textit{high-percentile} BSC slopes (90th and 75th percentiles) are most predictive deserves special attention. These percentiles capture the \textit{sharpest} portions of the boundary, which likely correspond to well-preserved primary sensorimotor cortices and other phylogenetically older cortical regions that are relatively spared until later disease stages. The "retrogenesis" or "last-in-first-out" hypothesis of cortical vulnerability in AD says that brain regions that myelinate last during development (association cortices, late-myelinating prefrontal regions) are most vulnerable to early degeneration, while primary sensorimotor areas that mature earliest are relatively preserved. Our results suggest that even in these preserved regions, \textit{subtle degradation of the sharpest boundaries} serves as a sensitive marker of disease progression. This may reflect that these high-percentile regions have the furthest to fall—they begin with the highest structural integrity and their degradation signals that disease processes have advanced significantly.

\subsection{Clinical Implications}

Our results have several translational applications with potential near-term clinical impact:

\textbf{Clinical trial enrichment} is probably the most immediate application. Current anti-amyloid trials in prodromal AD face big challenges in showing cognitive benefit, partly because many enrolled MCI patients progress slowly or not at all, diluting treatment effects. Enrichment strategies that find fast progressors can dramatically improve statistical power. For example, if a trial enrolls only subjects in the fastest BSC decline tercile (median conversion 2.1 years vs. 8.5 years for slow decliners), required sample size could be cut by 50-70\% while keeping equivalent power. This translates to big cost savings (Phase III trials in AD cost \$50-100 million) and faster time to regulatory approval. Moreover, showing efficacy in a high-risk enriched population may be ethically better than exposing slow progressors to experimental therapies with non-trivial adverse event profiles (amyloid-related imaging abnormalities, infusion reactions).

\textbf{Personalized risk stratification and monitoring} could enable precision medicine approaches to AD prevention. Subjects with rapid BSC decline could be prioritized for intensive surveillance (annual vs. biennial cognitive assessment), offered participation in lifestyle intervention programs (physical exercise, cognitive training, Mediterranean diet), or considered for off-label use of approved therapies. Conversely, subjects with stable BSC slopes might be reassured that their risk of near-term progression is low, reducing anxiety and unnecessary healthcare use. This stratification could be put into practice through machine learning-based clinical decision support systems built into radiology workflows, automatically flagging high-risk individuals from routine clinical MRI scans.

\textbf{Cost-effectiveness} is a critical consideration for real-world implementation. Structural MRI costs about \$800-1,500 per scan in the United States and is routinely done for clinical evaluation of cognitive complaints. In contrast, amyloid PET costs \$5,000-7,000 and is rarely covered by insurance outside research settings. CSF collection needs lumbar puncture, an invasive procedure with patient discomfort, contraindications in patients on anticoagulation, and risk of post-procedure headache. Blood-based biomarkers, while promising, aren't yet FDA-approved or widely available clinically. BSC analysis could be done as a post-processing step on clinical MRI scans without additional imaging time or cost, needing only computational infrastructure.

\textbf{Multimodal integration} offers the greatest potential for improved prediction. Combining BSC slopes with other prognostic markers—cognitive slopes (MMSE, ADAS-Cog), plasma biomarkers (p-tau217, NfL), APOE genotype, polygenic risk scores, and demographic variables—in ensemble models could get C-index values over 0.80, approaching clinically actionable thresholds. Recent studies combining structural MRI with amyloid PET and cognitive measures have reached AUC values of 0.85-0.90 for MCI conversion prediction. Our RSF framework naturally takes multimodal inputs and could serve as a foundation for such integration.

\textbf{Monitoring treatment response} is a future application as disease-modifying therapies become more widely available. If BSC degradation reflects core AD pathological processes (synaptic loss, tau buildup, neuroinflammation), then successful treatment should slow or halt BSC decline. Longitudinal BSC slopes could serve as surrogate endpoints in clinical trials, giving earlier readouts than clinical conversion (which may take 3-5 years of follow-up). This application needs validation through natural history studies showing that BSC slope changes predict later cognitive outcomes and that treatment-associated changes in BSC correlate with clinical benefit.

\subsection{Methodological Strengths}

This study benefits from several design features that boost validity and reproducibility. The \textbf{large longitudinal cohort} of 450 subjects with $\geq$4 scans per subject and mean 4.84-year follow-up (range: 0.5-18.4 years) gives good statistical power for detecting temporal effects and allows robust estimation of individual-level slopes. Requiring minimum four timepoints makes sure slopes aren't driven by two-point estimates, which are highly sensitive to measurement error and transient biological fluctuations. The extended follow-up duration captures both fast progressors (median conversion <2 years) and slow progressors (>8 years), spanning the full spectrum of disease trajectories.

\textbf{Temporal precision} was ensured by using actual MRI acquisition dates rather than nominal visit codes for slope computation. Many subjects in ADNI have scan timing variability (e.g., a "12-month" visit happening at 10 or 14 months), and using visit labels would add systematic error in slope estimates. By anchoring to acquisition dates, we accurately capture \textit{rates of change per year}, allowing direct biological interpretation.

\textbf{Voxel-level computation} of BSC maps gives spatially resolved information that goes beyond simple global summary statistics. While we combined to boundary-level features for statistical analysis, the voxel-wise maps preserve spatial heterogeneity and could allow future region-specific analyses using cortical parcellation schemes (e.g., Desikan-Killiany atlas).

\textbf{Robust preprocessing} standardized MRI data despite scanner heterogeneity (1.5T and 3T, multiple manufacturers). N4 bias correction removes intensity non-uniformity, Atropos segmentation gives consistent tissue classification across subjects, and artifact control (skull stripping, boundary band definition) minimizes including non-brain tissue or partial volume artifacts.

The \textbf{direct comparison} of baseline versus slopes on identical train/test splits and identical feature selection procedures makes sure performance evaluation is fair. Both models used the same 70/30 stratified split, removing the possibility that performance differences come from different evaluation sets. This within-subject comparison design (baseline features vs. slope features from the same individuals) gives a stringent test of our hypothesis.

\subsection{Limitations}

Several limitations should be acknowledged and guide interpretation of our findings. First, \textbf{single-cohort validation} means all results are based solely on ADNI participants, a research cohort with specific demographic characteristics (mostly white, highly educated, recruited from academic medical centers) and imaging protocols (high-quality research-grade MRI with standardized acquisition parameters). ADNI's strict quality control and relatively homogeneous demographics may not reflect real-world clinical populations, where scanner variability, motion artifacts, and diverse demographic backgrounds are the norm. Independent validation in OASIS, NACC, UK Biobank, and clinic-based cohorts representing broader populations is essential to confirm generalizability. Until such validation is done, our results should be considered preliminary.

Second, the \textbf{linear slope assumption} may not adequately capture the true complexity of disease progression. Neurodegeneration in AD likely follows nonlinear trajectories, potentially with periods of relative stability interrupted by acceleration ("inflection points") as compensatory mechanisms fail. Quadratic or piecewise linear models could better capture such dynamics, as could mixed-effects models allowing subject-specific random effects for both intercept and slope. Bayesian approaches could add prior knowledge about plausible trajectory shapes. We chose linear regression for interpretability and computational efficiency, but this simplification may miss important nonlinear patterns.

Third, \textbf{missing data and subject exclusion} may introduce selection bias. We needed $\geq$4 longitudinal scans for slope estimation, excluding subjects with fewer timepoints. These excluded subjects may differ systematically from included subjects (e.g., they may have progressed too rapidly to complete four scans, or dropped out due to frailty or other comorbidities). Modern imputation methods (multiple imputation, joint modeling of longitudinal and survival data) could potentially recover information from subjects with incomplete data, expanding the analysis sample and reducing selection bias.

Fourth, \textbf{scanner and sequence heterogeneity} exists within ADNI, which includes scans from both 1.5T and 3T systems across multiple manufacturers (Siemens, GE, Philips) and sites (>50 acquisition centers). While we used N4 bias correction to reduce field inhomogeneity, systematic differences in image contrast, resolution, and signal-to-noise ratio between scanner types may affect BSC measurements. Harmonization methods (ComBat, traveling phantom calibration, deep learning-based harmonization) could reduce this technical variability and improve signal detection.

Fifth, \textbf{feature selection} was done using variance rather than supervised methods. While high-variance features capture subjects with diverse trajectories, variance alone doesn't guarantee association with outcome. Supervised feature selection using univariate Cox regression, LASSO regularization, or recursive feature elimination might find a more parsimonious and predictive feature set. Our approach of picking the top 20 features by variance was pragmatic but not necessarily optimal. Also, we didn't explore feature engineering (interactions, polynomial terms, ratio features) that might improve performance.

Sixth, \textbf{overfitting} is evident from the substantial gap between training (C-index 0.84) and testing (C-index 0.63) performance. This 25\% relative drop suggests the Random Forest model memorized training-specific patterns. We did not employ cross-validation during training, did not tune hyperparameters systematically (number of trees, minimum samples per leaf, maximum depth), and did not implement ensemble methods beyond the single Random Forest. Future work should employ nested cross-validation, systematic hyperparameter optimization, and model averaging to improve generalization.

Seventh, \textbf{baseline AD exclusion} removed six subjects diagnosed with AD at their first scan. While these subjects can't contribute to MCI-to-AD conversion analysis in a standard survival framework (conversion time = 0 is undefined for log models), alternative approaches exist. We could have included them with a small $\epsilon$ time (e.g., 0.1 years), used left-truncation methods, or modeled them separately. This exclusion is a minor proportion of the cohort but may bias estimates if baseline AD subjects differ systematically from converters with positive event times.

Finally, \textbf{lack of external biomarker validation} means we can't definitively attribute BSC changes to specific pathological processes. While we hypothesize that BSC degradation reflects amyloid buildup, tau pathology, neuroinflammation, and synaptic loss, we didn't correlate BSC measures with PET imaging (amyloid, tau, microglial activation) or CSF biomarkers. Such multimodal validation studies would strengthen the biological interpretation and establish BSC's position within the amyloid/tau/neurodegeneration (ATN) framework.

\subsection{Future Directions}

Promising avenues for future research that would fix current limitations and extend these findings include:

\textbf{External validation and generalization} across diverse cohorts is the highest priority. Testing BSC slopes in OASIS-3 (open-access longitudinal neuroimaging), NACC-UDS (National Alzheimer's Coordinating Center Uniform Data Set, representing >40 AD centers across the US), UK Biobank (500,000+ participants with brain imaging), and clinic-based cohorts would check generalizability beyond ADNI's research setting. Special attention should be given to underrepresented populations (African American, Hispanic, Asian, low socioeconomic status) to make sure performance is equitable across demographic groups. Scanner-specific validation would measure the impact of imaging protocol variations on BSC measurements. If BSC slopes generalize successfully, this would support clinical translation; if not, it would identify specific populations or imaging contexts needing model recalibration.

\textbf{Multimodal integration} combining BSC slopes with complementary biomarker modalities offers the greatest potential for clinically actionable prediction accuracy. Cognitive slopes computed from serial MMSE, ADAS-Cog, or digit span testing capture functional decline orthogonal to structural imaging. Genetic risk scores including APOE genotype, polygenic risk scores combining genome-wide association study (GWAS) findings, and rare variant burden in APP, PSEN1, and PSEN2 genes measure inherited susceptibility. Blood biomarkers including plasma p-tau217 (strong correlation with brain tau PET), p-tau181, neurofilament light chain (NfL, marker of axonal injury), and glial fibrillary acidic protein (GFAP, astrocyte marker) give minimally invasive assessments of molecular pathology. Combining these modalities in stacked ensemble models or multimodal deep learning architectures could get C-index >0.80, approaching the threshold for clinical decision-making. The challenge is integrating data across heterogeneous sources and missing data patterns.

\textbf{Spatial mapping and regional specificity} would identify which brain regions' BSC slopes drive prediction. Automated parcellation using the Desikan-Killiany atlas (68 cortical regions), Destrieux atlas (148 regions), or functional network parcellations (Yeo 7-network, Schaefer 400-parcel) would allow region-specific slope estimation. We hypothesize that medial temporal lobe structures (entorhinal cortex, hippocampus, parahippocampal gyrus), posterior cingulate cortex, and precuneus—regions showing early tau buildup in Braak staging—would show the steepest BSC decline in converters. Conversely, primary sensorimotor cortex should show relative preservation. Regional analysis could also test the "epicenter" hypothesis that AD pathology spreads from specific starting sites through anatomically connected networks. Voxel-based morphometry or connectome-based predictive modeling could identify distributed patterns of boundary degradation across networks.

\textbf{Biological subtype analysis} acknowledging AD heterogeneity could improve prediction within subgroups. APOE genotype stratification (ε4 carriers vs. non-carriers) is straightforward given ADNI genetic data availability. ATN (Amyloid/Tau/Neurodegeneration) biomarker profiling using PET imaging would distinguish subjects with high amyloid but low tau (preclinical AD, slow progression) from those with high amyloid and high tau (prodromal AD, fast progression). Suspected non-Alzheimer's pathology (SNAP) subjects showing neurodegeneration without amyloid buildup may have distinct BSC trajectories. Data-driven clustering of multimodal biomarkers could find novel subtypes with different prognosis and potentially different treatment responses.

\textbf{Deep learning end-to-end prediction} bypassing manual feature engineering could use the full information content of voxel-wise BSC maps. Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) applied to 3D BSC volumes could learn hierarchical spatial representations, while recurrent neural networks (RNNs, LSTMs) or transformers could model temporal dynamics across the longitudinal sequence. Attention mechanisms could find spatiotemporal patterns most predictive of conversion. However, deep learning needs larger sample sizes than currently available (typically >1,000 subjects for stable training) and faces interpretability challenges. Explainable AI methods (saliency maps, integrated gradients, attention visualization) would be essential for clinical acceptance.

\textbf{Treatment response prediction and monitoring} is a future application contingent on broader therapeutic availability. As anti-amyloid antibodies (lecanemab, donanemab) and potentially anti-tau therapies reach clinical practice, biomarkers that predict treatment response will be valuable. Does rapid baseline BSC decline identify subjects most (or least) likely to benefit from amyloid clearance? Do successful treatments slow BSC decline? Longitudinal BSC slopes could serve as pharmacodynamic markers in early-phase trials, giving mechanistic evidence that a drug engages its target (e.g., slowed neurodegeneration). Combining BSC with amyloid PET could separate amyloid removal (direct drug effect) from downstream neuroprotection (clinical benefit).

\textbf{Preclinical detection} extending this framework to cognitively normal individuals at risk (amyloid+/tau+ by PET, APOE ε4 homozygotes, autosomal dominant mutation carriers) could enable the earliest possible intervention. Can BSC slopes predict symptom onset 5-10 years in advance? The Anti-Amyloid Treatment in Asymptomatic Alzheimer's (A4) trial and related studies give cohorts for such analysis. Primary prevention trials will need biomarkers that work in preclinical stages where cognitive measures aren't sensitive enough.

\section{Conclusion}

Longitudinal slopes of the Boundary Sharpness Coefficient (BSC), analyzed via Random Survival Forest, work much better than baseline BSC measurements for predicting MCI-to-AD conversion. In a cohort of 450 ADNI subjects with $\geq$4 scans (mean follow-up: 4.84 years), the RSF slope-based model got a test C-index of 0.63, a 163\% improvement over baseline parametric models (C-index: 0.24). Key predictors included boundary voxel count slope (Nboundary\_slope: variance = 3.16M), magnitude 90th percentile slope (bsc\_mag\_p90\_slope: variance = 0.0102), and magnitude 75th percentile slope (bsc\_mag\_p75\_slope: variance = 0.0073), with converters showing accelerated degradation over time.

These findings support the hypothesis that \textit{temporal biomarker trajectories} capture disease progression dynamics more effectively than static measurements. The dramatic failure of baseline models (C-index worse than chance) confirms that cross-sectional BSC doesn't predict well, while slopes reveal critical progression signals. BSC slopes offer a cost-effective, non-invasive alternative to amyloid PET or CSF biomarkers, with potential applications in clinical trial enrichment, personalized risk stratification, and multimodal prognostic models.

Important limitations include modest test performance (C-index: 0.63) relative to training (C-index: 0.84), suggesting overfitting and the need for larger validation cohorts. Future work should (1) validate in independent datasets (OASIS, NACC, UK Biobank), (2) combine BSC slopes with cognitive scores and blood biomarkers to improve performance, (3) explore ensemble methods and regularization to reduce overfitting, and (4) investigate regional and subtype-specific degradation patterns. As the field moves toward precision medicine for AD, measuring \textit{rates of change} in neuroimaging biomarkers will be critical for early detection, monitoring, and intervention.
