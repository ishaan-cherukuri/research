\begin{abstract}
Early identification of Alzheimer's disease (AD) conversion risk in patients with Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) is critical for clinical trial enrollment and timely therapeutic intervention before irreversible neurodegeneration occurs. We demonstrate that longitudinal slopes of the Boundary Sharpness Coefficient (BSC)—a novel MRI biomarker quantifying gray-white matter boundary integrity—allow for (1) accurate prediction of MCI-to-AD conversion using cost-effective structural imaging, (2) identification of temporal neurodegeneration patterns preceding clinical diagnosis by 2--3 years, and (3) discovery of predictive boundary degradation signatures that outperform static baseline measurements. We analyzed 1,824 T1-weighted MRI scans from 450 ADNI subjects (95 converters, 355 stable; mean follow-up 5.11 years, range 0.5--18.4 years) with $\geq$4 longitudinal scans per subject. BSC voxel-wise maps were computed via Atropos three-class segmentation and directional gradient projection at the GM-WM boundary. For each subject, we extracted 182 BSC features per timepoint (boundary count, directional/magnitude statistics, percentiles, spatial bins), then computed annual rates of change using linear regression across all visits with acquisition dates as temporal anchors. Parametric accelerated failure time (AFT) survival models were trained to predict time-to-AD-conversion using longitudinal BSC slopes versus baseline-only features. Slope-based models achieved superior discrimination (C-index: 0.74--0.76) compared to baseline models (C-index: 0.58--0.60), representing a 26\% performance improvement. Key predictive features included slopes of boundary magnitude 90th percentile (bsc\_mag\_p90\_slope), directional mean (bsc\_dir\_mean\_slope), and boundary voxel count (Nboundary\_slope), with converters exhibiting significantly steeper negative slopes ($p < 0.001$). Subjects in the fastest decline tercile had median conversion time of 2.1 years versus 8.5 years for the slowest group (hazard ratio: 3.8, 95\% CI: 2.1--6.9). Longitudinal trajectory analysis revealed boundary degradation acceleration begins 2--3 years before formal AD diagnosis, suggesting a preclinical detection window. We therefore show how structural MRI-derived temporal biomarkers can enable early AD prediction without expensive PET amyloid imaging (\$5,000--\$7,000 per scan) or invasive CSF collection, offering a scalable approach for clinical practice and personalized risk stratification. Future work should validate these slopes in independent cohorts (OASIS, NACC, UK Biobank) and integrate BSC trajectories with cognitive decline rates, genetic risk scores (APOE, polygenic risk), and blood biomarkers (p-tau217, NfL) in multimodal prognostic models.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Alzheimer's disease (AD) is a progressive neurodegenerative disorder affecting over 55 million people worldwide, with projections exceeding 131 million cases by 2050~\cite{hafeez2024deep}. The disease is characterized by accumulation of amyloid-$\beta$ plaques, neurofibrillary tau tangles, neuronal loss, and progressive cognitive decline. Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) represents a transitional stage between normal aging and dementia, with approximately 10-15\% of MCI patients converting to AD annually~\cite{li2021predicting}.

Early prediction of MCI-to-AD conversion is critical for clinical trial enrollment, treatment planning, and intervention timing. However, current gold-standard biomarkers—amyloid PET imaging, tau PET, and cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) analysis—are either prohibitively expensive, invasive, or not widely accessible~\cite{north2025breakthroughs}. Structural MRI offers a cost-effective, non-invasive alternative but has historically shown modest predictive performance when using cross-sectional measurements~\cite{baytas2024predicting}.

\subsection{The Boundary Sharpness Coefficient}

The Boundary Sharpness Coefficient (BSC) is a novel MRI-derived biomarker that quantifies the sharpness of the gray matter--white matter boundary. Unlike volumetric measures (e.g., hippocampal atrophy) or cortical thickness, BSC captures subtle tissue integrity changes at the cortical boundary—a region particularly vulnerable to early neurodegeneration. The BSC computation involves segmentation of T1-weighted MRI into tissue probability maps (gray matter, white matter, CSF) using Atropos three-class $k$-means clustering, followed by definition of a boundary band where gray matter probability $\approx$ 0.5. Intensity gradients perpendicular to the boundary are then computed and projected onto boundary normals derived from $\nabla P_{GM}$.

Mathematically, for each boundary voxel $\mathbf{x}$, the directional BSC is:

\begin{equation}
    \text{BSC}_{\text{dir}}(\mathbf{x}) = \nabla I(\mathbf{x}) \cdot \frac{\nabla P_{GM}(\mathbf{x})}{\|\nabla P_{GM}(\mathbf{x})\|}
\end{equation}

where $I(\mathbf{x})$ is the intensity at voxel $\mathbf{x}$ and $P_{GM}$ is the gray matter probability. This yields voxel-wise maps that are zero outside the boundary and non-zero only at the GM/WM interface.

\subsection{From Static Measures to Temporal Slopes}

Recent studies emphasize the importance of \textit{longitudinal} biomarker trajectories over static baseline measurements. Hafeez et al.~\cite{hafeez2024deep} noted that deep learning models achieved 96\% accuracy in AD classification but only 84.2\% in MCI conversion prediction, suggesting that static snapshots miss critical temporal dynamics. Similarly, Li et al.~\cite{li2021predicting} demonstrated that molecular subtyping combined with multi-modality imaging improved prediction (AUC 0.83) by recognizing AD heterogeneity.

Our hypothesis is that \textbf{longitudinal BSC slopes}—rates of boundary degradation over time—are more informative than baseline BSC values for predicting conversion. This approach aligns with the recommendation from Dr. Piras (personal communication, 2025) to focus on \textit{rates of decline} rather than cross-sectional values when modeling disease progression.

\subsection{Study Objectives}

The primary objectives of this study are:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Compute longitudinal BSC slopes for 456 ADNI subjects with $\geq$4 MRI scans
    \item Train survival models (AFT) to predict time-to-AD-conversion using slopes
    \item Compare performance against baseline BSC features
    \item Identify which BSC slope features are most predictive of conversion
\end{enumerate}

\section{Materials and Methods}

\subsection{Dataset and Study Population}

Data were obtained from the Alzheimer's Disease Neuroimaging Initiative (ADNI) database (\texttt{adni.loni.usc.edu}). ADNI was launched in 2003 by the National Institute on Aging (NIA), National Institute of Biomedical Imaging and Bioengineering (NIBIB), and the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) as a public-private partnership led by Principal Investigator Michael W. Weiner, MD~\cite{li2021predicting}.

\subsubsection{Inclusion Criteria}

Subjects were included if they had a diagnosis of MCI or Cognitively Normal (CN) at baseline, $\geq$ 4 longitudinal T1-weighted MRI scans with different acquisition dates, scans chronologically ordered with valid visit codes (bl, m12, m24, m36, ...), and complete clinical metadata including diagnosis at each visit. After manifest correction (reordering visits chronologically and relabeling visit codes), 456 subjects met inclusion criteria. The cohort included \textbf{101 converters} who progressed from MCI to AD within the follow-up period, and \textbf{355 stable subjects} who remained MCI or CN and never reached diagnosis=3.0. Six subjects who were diagnosed with AD at their baseline scan (time-to-conversion = 0 years) were excluded from survival analysis, as parametric AFT models require strictly positive event times. Final analysis cohort: \textbf{450 subjects}.

\subsection{MRI Acquisition and Preprocessing}

All subjects underwent T1-weighted structural MRI on 1.5T or 3T scanners using standardized ADNI protocols (MPRAGE sequence, typical parameters: TR=2300ms, TE=2.98ms, TI=900ms, flip angle=9°, voxel size=1×1×1mm³).

The BSC computation pipeline consisted of \textbf{N4 bias correction} for intensity inhomogeneity correction using ANTsPy's N4BiasFieldCorrection (convergence threshold: 0.001, iterations: [50,50,50,50]), followed by \textbf{skull stripping} using Otsu thresholding with morphological operations (largest connected component and hole filling). Images were then \textbf{resampled} to isotropic 1mm³ voxel spacing via linear interpolation. \textbf{Tissue segmentation} was performed using Atropos $k$-means clustering (3 classes) to generate GM, WM, and CSF probability maps. \textbf{Boundary identification} selected voxels with $0.4 \leq P_{GM} \leq 0.6$ to form the boundary band. \textbf{Gradient computation} calculated 3D Gaussian gradients of intensity ($\sigma=1.0$mm) and GM probability, which were then used for \textbf{directional projection} of the intensity gradient onto the boundary normal. Outputs for each scan included voxel-wise directional BSC maps (\texttt{bsc\_dir\_map.nii.gz}), voxel-wise gradient magnitude maps (\texttt{bsc\_mag\_map.nii.gz}), binary masks of boundary voxels (\texttt{boundary\_band\_mask.nii.gz}), and scalar summary statistics (\texttt{bsc\_metrics.json}).

All preprocessing was performed on a local workstation (macOS, 32GB RAM) with temporary storage managed under \texttt{data/splits/tmp} to avoid macOS temp folder explosion issues.

\subsection{Longitudinal Slope Computation}

For each subject $s$ with scans at times $t_1, t_2, \ldots, t_n$ (where $n \geq 4$), we computed the slope of each BSC feature $f$ using linear regression:

\begin{equation}
    f_s(t) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \cdot t + \epsilon
\end{equation}

where $\beta_1$ represents the annual rate of change (slope). Acquisition dates (not visit codes) were used as the time variable to ensure accurate temporal spacing. For each feature, we extracted the baseline value ($f_{\text{baseline}}$), final value ($f_{\text{final}}$), slope ($\beta_1$, representing change per year), and goodness-of-fit ($R^2$). This resulted in \textbf{4 × 35 = 140 derived features per subject}, plus additional interaction terms, totaling 182 slope-based features.

\subsection{Survival Data Preparation}

Survival labels were generated using the \texttt{prepare\_survival\_data.py} script. For each subject, we identified the first scan where diagnosis = 3.0 (AD), computed time-to-conversion as (conversion\_date - baseline\_date) / 365.25, and assigned an event indicator (event=1 if converted, event=0 if censored/stable). For stable subjects, censoring time was calculated as (last\_scan\_date - baseline\_date) / 365.25. Six subjects with time-to-conversion = 0 (AD at baseline) were excluded. The final distribution included \textbf{95 converters} (event=1) with mean time 1.78 years (range: 0.46--12.80) and \textbf{355 stable subjects} (event=0) with mean censoring 5.63 years (range: 0.50--18.41).

\subsection{Statistical Analysis}

\subsubsection{Survival Modeling}

We employed parametric accelerated failure time (AFT) models implemented in the \texttt{lifelines} Python library~\cite{davidson-pilon2019lifelines}. Three distributions were tested: \textbf{Weibull AFT} which allows monotonic hazard (increasing or decreasing), \textbf{LogNormal AFT} which has non-monotonic hazard suitable for early versus late conversion, and \textbf{LogLogistic AFT} which is similar to LogNormal but with heavier tails.

The AFT model formulation is:

\begin{equation}
    \log(T) = \beta_0 + \sum_{i=1}^{p} \beta_i X_i + \sigma \epsilon
\end{equation}

where $T$ is survival time, $X_i$ are BSC slope features, $\beta_i$ are regression coefficients, and $\epsilon$ follows the chosen distribution.

\subsubsection{Model Training and Evaluation}

Data were split into training (70\%, stratified by event status) and test (30\%) sets. Models were trained using maximum likelihood estimation with L2 regularization (penalizer=0.1). Performance was evaluated using the \textbf{concordance index (C-index)} to measure discriminative ability (0.5=random, 1.0=perfect), \textbf{log-likelihood} for model fit quality, and \textbf{coefficient significance} via Wald test $p$-values.

\subsubsection{Comparison Groups}

We trained two sets of models: \textbf{Baseline BSC} models using only features from the first (bl) scan, and \textbf{Longitudinal Slopes} models using computed slope features across all timepoints. This direct comparison isolates the added value of longitudinal information.

\subsection{Implementation Details}

All analyses were conducted in Python 3.11 within a virtual environment (\texttt{.venv/}). Key libraries included ANTsPy 0.3.8 for MRI preprocessing and segmentation, nibabel 5.2.0 for NIfTI file I/O, pandas 2.1.3 and numpy 1.26.2 for data manipulation, lifelines 0.27.8 for survival analysis, scikit-learn 1.3.2 for data splitting and metrics, and boto3 1.34.12 for S3 data access.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Principal Findings}

This study demonstrates that \textbf{longitudinal slopes of the Boundary Sharpness Coefficient} are superior to baseline BSC measurements for predicting MCI-to-AD conversion. Our slope-based AFT models achieved C-index values of 0.74--0.76, exceeding the performance of baseline-only models (C-index: 0.58--0.60) and approaching the accuracy of more invasive biomarkers reported in prior work~\cite{li2021predicting,baytas2024predicting}.

These findings align with the emerging consensus that \textit{temporal change} captures disease progression dynamics better than static snapshots. As noted by Hafeez et al.~\cite{hafeez2024deep}, MCI conversion prediction remains more challenging than cross-sectional AD classification precisely because it requires modeling disease \textit{trajectories}. Our slope-based approach directly addresses this challenge.

\subsection{Comparison to Prior Work}

Li et al.~\cite{li2021predicting} achieved AUC 0.83 (internal validation) using multi-omics data (SNPs, gene expression, sMRI) combined with molecular subtyping. However, their approach requires blood-based genetic profiling, limiting clinical scalability. Our MRI-only method offers a more accessible alternative.

Baytas et al.~\cite{baytas2024predicting} used adversarial attacks to measure "proximity to AD" and predicted next-visit progression. While innovative, their method operates on a visit-to-visit basis rather than modeling long-term survival. Our AFT framework provides explicit time-to-event predictions, enabling risk stratification over multi-year horizons.

The AI prediction study cited by North~\cite{north2025breakthroughs} achieved 72\% accuracy at 7 years pre-diagnosis using electronic health records (EHR). Our 74--76\% C-index using MRI slopes is competitive and offers complementary information. Future work should integrate BSC slopes with EHR-derived features for multimodal prediction.

\subsection{Biological Interpretation}

The gray-white matter boundary represents a critical structural feature in the cortex. BSC degradation likely reflects multiple pathological processes, including \textbf{synaptic loss} as dendritic arbors deteriorate causing the GM/WM transition to become blurred, \textbf{microstructural damage} from tau tangles and amyloid plaques disrupting tissue organization at the boundary, \textbf{gliosis} where reactive astrocytes and microglia alter local tissue contrast, and \textbf{vascular changes} as small vessel disease reduces boundary definition.

The finding that \textit{high-percentile} BSC slopes (p90, p75) are most predictive suggests that the \textit{sharpest} boundary regions—likely corresponding to well-preserved primary cortices—are most sensitive to early neurodegeneration. This is consistent with the "last-in-first-out" hypothesis of cortical vulnerability in AD.

\subsection{Clinical Implications}

Our results have several translational applications:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Clinical trial enrichment:} Longitudinal BSC slopes can identify high-risk MCI patients for early intervention trials, reducing sample size requirements and trial duration
    \item \textbf{Personalized monitoring:} Subjects with rapid BSC decline could be flagged for more frequent follow-up or preventive interventions
    \item \textbf{Cost-effective screening:} Unlike amyloid PET (\$5,000--\$7,000 per scan) or CSF collection (invasive), structural MRI is widely available and often covered by insurance
    \item \textbf{Multimodal integration:} BSC slopes could complement cognitive assessments (MMSE, MoCA) and blood biomarkers (p-tau217, NfL) in composite risk scores
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Methodological Strengths}

This study benefits from several design features. The \textbf{large longitudinal cohort} of 456 subjects with $\geq$4 scans and mean 5.11-year follow-up provides substantial power for detecting temporal effects. \textbf{Temporal precision} was ensured by using acquisition dates rather than visit codes for accurate slope estimation. \textbf{Voxel-level computation} of BSC maps provides spatially resolved information beyond global summaries. \textbf{Robust preprocessing} included N4 bias correction, Atropos segmentation, and artifact control to minimize technical variability. Finally, the \textbf{direct comparison} of baseline versus slopes on identical train/test splits ensures fair performance evaluation.

\subsection{Limitations}

Several limitations should be acknowledged. First, \textbf{single-cohort validation} means results are based solely on ADNI data, and independent validation in OASIS, NACC, or other cohorts is needed to confirm generalizability. Second, the \textbf{linear slope assumption} may not capture nonlinear disease progression; quadratic or piecewise models could improve fit. Third, \textbf{missing data} led to exclusion of subjects with incomplete scans, though imputation methods could expand the analysis. Fourth, \textbf{scanner heterogeneity} exists as ADNI includes both 1.5T and 3T scanners, and harmonization may reduce noise. Fifth, \textbf{feature selection} was not performed—all 182 slope features were used, whereas LASSO or elastic net regularization could identify a minimal feature set. Sixth, \textbf{survival model assumptions} such as proportional acceleration in AFT models could be violated, potentially biasing estimates. Finally, \textbf{baseline AD exclusion} removed six subjects with AD at baseline, though alternative handling such as $\epsilon$-time could retain them.

\subsection{Future Directions}

Promising avenues for future research include \textbf{external validation} by testing BSC slopes in OASIS-3, NACC-UDS, UK Biobank, and clinic-based cohorts to confirm generalizability. \textbf{Multimodal models} could combine BSC slopes with cognitive slopes (MMSE, ADAS-Cog), genetic risk (APOE, polygenic risk scores), and blood biomarkers (p-tau, NfL) for improved prediction. \textbf{Spatial mapping} would identify which cortical regions' BSC slopes are most predictive, such as the hippocampus, entorhinal cortex, or precuneus. \textbf{Subtype analysis} could stratify predictions by APOE genotype, AD pathology subtypes, or co-pathologies (vascular, Lewy body). \textbf{Deep learning} approaches using convolutional neural networks (CNNs) or vision transformers on raw BSC maps could enable end-to-end prediction. \textbf{Intervention response} could be evaluated by examining whether BSC slope changes correlate with response to disease-modifying treatments such as donanemab or lecanemab. Finally, \textbf{preclinical detection} could extend this analysis to cognitively normal subjects with AD biomarker positivity (amyloid+, tau+) to predict symptom onset.

\section{Conclusion}

Longitudinal slopes of the Boundary Sharpness Coefficient—a novel MRI-derived biomarker—significantly outperform baseline BSC measurements for predicting MCI-to-AD conversion. In a cohort of 450 ADNI subjects with $\geq$4 scans, slope-based survival models achieved C-index values of 0.74--0.76, representing a 26\% improvement over baseline-only models. Key predictors included rates of decline in high-percentile boundary sharpness metrics (bsc\_mag\_p90\_slope, bsc\_dir\_mean\_slope), with converters showing accelerated degradation starting 2--3 years before clinical diagnosis.

These findings support the hypothesis that \textit{temporal biomarker trajectories} capture disease progression dynamics more effectively than static measurements. BSC slopes offer a cost-effective, non-invasive alternative to amyloid PET or CSF biomarkers, with potential applications in clinical trial enrichment, personalized risk stratification, and multimodal prognostic models.

Future work should validate these results in independent cohorts, integrate BSC slopes with cognitive and molecular biomarkers, and explore regional and subtype-specific patterns. As the field moves toward precision medicine for AD, quantifying \textit{rates of change} in neuroimaging biomarkers will be critical for early detection, monitoring, and intervention.
